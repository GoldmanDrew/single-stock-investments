Portfolio Update 
 
In general, the first half reporting season has shown that our portfolio companies have 
been trading (operationally) in line with our expectations.  We have been in dialogue, 
wherever possible, with management teams of our companies to check their outlook for 
their businesses and strategic direction. 
 
Gazprom 
 
On August 18 Gazprom local shares declined 13% to 53 rubles due to a request from a 
Duma member for an investigation into Gazprom structures facilitating foreign 
investment in local shares. Our assessment is that this Duma member is politically 
unimportant and, according to market sources, had other motives.  We believe that 
nothing significant will result from the comments.  This was confirmed in a dinner which 
I attended on September 8th with Oleg Vyugin, the Head of the Federal Service for 
Financial Markets, the key market regulator, who confirmed that there was no 
investigation at any level of Government into Gazprom share ownership by foreigners 
and none was planned.  He recently publicly reaffirmed his position that these structures 
are legal.

On September 14th President Putin and Prime Minister Fradkov announced publicly that 
the Russian government would increase its ownership of Gazprom from 38% to 50% + 1 
share through an exchange of Gazprom treasury stock for the state owned oil company, 
Rosneft, to be followed by the removal of all foreign ownership restrictions on Gazprom. 
The timetable given for the Rosneft acquisition is completion by year end 2004. These 
statements resulted in the local shares rallying to 73 rubles. 
 
During September we met in London with the Gazprom CFO. At the meeting he stated 
that the management fully supported the elimination of the ring fence and the ending of 
all restrictions on foreign ownership so that there would be ‘one price for all Gazprom 
shares’.  One important argument for the ring fence removal for the management was a 
desire to improve their market capitalization and hence their credit rating in order to 
reduce their cost of funding and increase debt capacity.  The lifting of the ring fence will 
also result in the domestic stock entering into long only benchmarks such as MSCI EMF, 
creating a new cohort of buyers. The meeting highlighted the many shortfalls of the 
company (relative to western standards) on the issues of capital budgeting, cost 
discipline, financial targets, and shareholder rather than social purpose focus. The 
company reported Q1 figures during September which were positive in that the company 
generated over $1.3bn of free cash flow. 
 
Our position is currently long 10% of NAV and net long 5% (net of our GDR short).  We 
would expect the current 40-50% premium of the Gazprom GDR to close to near parity if 
the ring fence is genuinely removed and both shares are made fungible.  We view the 
position as an event driven arbitrage rather than a fundamental investment.  We currently 
hold material excess borrow in the GDR to increase our hedge ratio over time. 
 
LG Corp 
 
During the quarter LG Corp completed its demerger into GS Holdings and new LG Corp.  
This combined value of the two stocks is double where we first invested in the company.  
We have sold our GS Holdings position due to inadequate mispricing on a mid cycle 
valuation although earnings momentum is very good for the company.  We increased our 
holding in the new LG Corp due to the compelling valuation of a 55% discount to our 
NAV estimate and PE of 3X our 2005 earnings estimate.  The main risk continues to be 
the negative impact on LG Electronics’ earnings if the Korean Won strengthens.  This is 
currently a 6% gross long and 4% net long position (net of our 2% hedge of their 
proportionate ownership of the newly listed LG Phillips).

Italian Banks 
 
We currently hold long positions in three Italian banks – Unicredito, Banca Intesa (non-
voting shares), and Banco Popolare di Verona.  All have now reported H1 in line with our 
expectations.  On average we think their average P/E of approximately 9X our 2005 
earnings estimates, expected 2005 dividend yields of 5%+, and optionality on short term 
rate rises make these attractive investments given their potential for double digit earnings 
growth.  We have recently met with the top management of all three banks during the 
quarter and heard a consistent message of growth in spite of a flat economy. 
 
Autostrade 
 
During the quarter the company’s controlling shareholder sold down its shareholding 
from 63% to 51% through the sale of nearly $1bn of stock at 16 euros.  We participated 
in the placing.  H1 trading was in line with our expectations and we have increased our 
comfort level on long term regulatory risks through more detailed analysis with the 
company and external legal advisors on the contractual terms of their investment plan.  
Over the long term the company is in a unique position to continue investing at attractive 
rates of return with low risk in de-bottlenecking projects, in effect acting as a banker to 
the Italian government for infrastructure investments. The value created by the 
releveraging of the company is still not reflected in the stock price despite the strong 
increase in valuation, partly because most long only funds sold all their shares during the 
tender offer in 2003 at 10 euros and have been reluctant to look again at the company. 
 
KT&G 
 
We increased our position during the quarter through a placing of shares by a large 
domestic shareholder.  We see the company as highly undervalued at approximately 4x 
our estimated EV/ operating profit.  Over the medium term we see tax rises being 
absorbed by the company’s pricing power.  The company is currently engaged in a 
buyback program to repurchase 24% of its shares by 2007.  The one negative that we see 
is that up to 10% of these shares are not slated for cancellation but partly for management 
and employee remuneration.  We see this as very excessive and are considering taking an 
activist approach to persuade the board to cancel more of the repurchased shares. 
 
TUI 
 
Our short position on TUI illustrates the problems which the proliferation of hedge funds 
have caused in short positions in anything but the large cap index names. When we first 
executed our short position on the stock borrow was freely available and the cost 65bp.  
Our prime broker was lending to us primarily exclusive stock (ie. stock in which they had 
agreed a fixed fee and certainty of supply for a fixed period of a year) as opposed to spot 
market borrow where no such restrictions apply.  In subsequent months this short position 
has become popular amongst hedge funds and the prime brokers have shown no restraint 
in offering every last available share out until significant recalls and repricings (the spot

Assets Under Management 
During the quarter the Master Fund remained closed to new investors.  However, we have 
continued to find some attractive investment opportunities in several areas which we 
believe will comfortably exceed the Fund’s target rates of return. As a result we have 
now taken the Fund’s leverage to the upper end of what we feel comfortable with.  At this 
point in time we would like to deleverage the Fund by raising approximately $100-200m 
of capital. 
We clearly understand that many investors want us to manage as little capital as possible 
so as to increase the return that they make on their capital.  I too, as a significant 
shareholder in the Fund, do not want our returns diluted.  Moreover, we understand that 
ultimately we will be fired by our investors if we take more capital than we can profitably 
invest at (a) the target returns which the fund set out at the beginning (10% pa with 
drawdown targets of less than 10% of capital) and (b) the target returns of our investors 
(stated to be, in general, similar to our own).  The proof of this pudding will, as always, 
be in the eating.  Moreover, if and when we find the attractive opportunity set to be 
materially less than our capital, base we intend to return capital to our investors.
