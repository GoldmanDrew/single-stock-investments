The fund ended the quarter down 0.6% gross of fees. During the quarter the European 
markets fell by up to 16% from the highs to lows triggered by rising global interest 
rates, commodity price rises and geopolitical concerns. Many emerging markets, 
including Brazil, India and Mexico, where we have substantial exposures, fell by 20% or 
more. As a result we experienced a large drawdown in May and early June which was 
partly recovered during the second half of June. As a long biased fund with leverage and 
exposures to emerging markets it is not a surprise to us that we were hit hard by these 
market falls, especially given our strong first quarter returns. 
 
Overall there were no dramatic changes in the composition of the portfolio during the 
quarter. We increased our short exposure with stock specific shorts (and for a short time 
with index hedges) by some 6%. Most of the increase in the short book has been in the 
telecom and technology space in the US and Japan where we have been able to find 
shorts with a strong risk reward. Today the Fund is long 122% in outright longs and 
short 15% in stock specific shorts (one of which is hedged with a directly owned asset). 
Our net long is 108%. Today we have no index shorts. Our long book increased 
exposures in some basic materials stocks – Arcelor (now Mittal), Arcelor Brazil and 
Cemex.   
 
During the quarter many hedge funds substantially reduced market exposures and 
delevered, perhaps exacerbating the market falls. We chose not to do this because the 
corrections had made the portfolio very cheap in our view. By-mid June, the vast 
majority of the portfolio showed an upside of over 50% to fair value on our analysis, 
with almost nothing less than 30%. That said, it was not a great feeling to have our 
worst daily and monthly performance, since we started the fund, coincide with our 
investor day.   
 
We have no crystal ball forecasting whether the stock markets of the world will collapse 
and take down our investments with them – part of this will depend on fundamental 
factors such as the extent to which inflationary pressures in the world translate into 
higher interest rates. Certainly there are serious market risks.  However, from a market 
valuation perspective we find European equities undervalued (perhaps by 20% or so) at 
circa 12x P/E on 2007 consensus EPS. European economies are doing fine and long bond 
yields in Europe at 4% may be sustainable because of high European unemployment and 
a balanced trade account. Of course, valuation is but one of the factors that drives 
markets in the short- and medium-term.   
 
Our strategy will be to continue with our philosophy of finding long ideas in strong 
businesses with big value gaps and step up our search for compelling short ideas which 
we believe can make money over the long-term in any market environment. We 
continue to encourage our investors not to focus on our short-term or relative results – 
two of the great problems of investment life – but our long-term absolute investment 
record and our process. Volatility will be an opportunity over the long run if discipline is 
practiced to hold investments with sufficient margin of safety. Barton Biggs, in his book 
Hedgehogging, summarises it well when he says “patience is a virtue, but it is tough (for 
investors) to practice.’’   
 
On the short side one of our considerations is that because the lending margin allowed 
by prime brokers is constrained for large concentrated long positions, we do not have 
the capital to run a large stock specific short book at the current time. The downside of 
shorting index futures is our ability to add alpha is limited, and the correlation risk with 
our long book is material. So far an inability to find sufficient short ideas that meet our 
hurdle rates has been the constraint on the short side but going forward capital has 
become a constraint.

4. Portfolio Update 
 
Deutsche Boerse and Euronext 
 
At the May AGM of Euronext, there was a vote proposed by a TCI investment company 
on the principle of a merger with Deutsche Boerse. 38% of voting shareholders voted in 
favour of this proposal and 62% against.  The board of Euronext shortly thereafter 
agreed a takeover of the company by the New York Stock Exchange for approximately 
€70 per share in stock and cash. This deal is subject to majority approval by 
shareholders.  
 
Deutsche Boerse has made a specific proposal to merge with Euronext for cash and 
shares with approximately 42% of the combined company’s stock going to Euronext 
shareholders, depending on how the stocks trade during an averaging period. They have 
modified their original proposal to offer the chairmanship of the merged company to 
Euronext with the CEO position going to Deutsche Boerse and offered equal board 
representation to Euronext as well as a willingness to use the Euronext IT system for the 
cash markets.   
 
The French government is studying the two merger proposals but has publicly stated its 
preference for a European solution. This has been echoed by Trichet, the head of the 
European Central Bank. We believe that the concessions on governance offered by 
Deutsche Boerse will make the French stakeholders favour a European solution because 
they will have more influence over the way the company is run than under a NYSE deal.  
Moreover, European companies are concerned over the potential of being dragged into 
the net of SEC regulation. 
 
Between the French banks, Axa and the user group of French companies, it is estimated 
that there is a combined 15-20% of the shares held. In addition, we believe that over 
25% of the stock is held by large common shareholders of Deutsche Boerse and

Euronext who would benefit more from a merger of the two companies. Given this we 
still believe that there is at least a 50% probability of the Deutsche Boerse merger 
despite the CEO and Chairman of Euronext being hostile to this deal. The key will be 
seeing if the French shareholders are willing, post the French government study, to 
formally and publicly support the Deutsche Boerse merger. 
  
Link REIT 
 
Link reported its maiden set of results for the four months ending March 2006. Despite it 
being too early for any management actions to really come through in the numbers 
(they have three-year leasing cycles), they were encouraging because they showed rent 
increases of over 6% based solely on reversionary pricing without any change in tenant 
mix or active management of leases. The dividend was raised ten per cent to imply a 
4% dividend yield. The analytical work that we have done gives us high confidence in 
the value of the REIT being materially higher than the current stock price. Whilst we 
think that the management team is on the right track with their strategy, we seek a 
more aggressive program of change than the board is currently supporting. As an 18.5% 
shareholder of the company (the largest by far) we think that we have a lot of power to 
get the results that we seek over time. We have recently requested one to two board 
seats and an upgrading of the board composition.     
 
Cemex 
 
The position in Cemex fell sharply in price during the quarter as investors sold the 
Mexican Bolsa aggressively. We used the weakness to increase our position which now 
represents 7% of NAV. It is our belief that the company trades on a 12% free cash flow 
yield and is growing at above GDP rates net of the capex we assume in this yield and 
has world class management.  
 
In Europe and the US, which represent two thirds of profits, we believe that there are 
high barriers to entry in getting permits to build new cement plants due to 
environmental considerations. We think the US is a tight market with 30% of needs 
currently being met by low margin imports from the existing producers. In the event of 
a slowdown in the US, these imports will be cut. When we recently met the CFO of the 
company in London, he confirmed that their guidance of $7 per share of free cash flow 
assumed a slowdown in certain markets which had not yet occurred. We continue to see 
no strong reason why the stock should trade at a 30% plus discount to its global 
competitors Lafarge and Holcim. 
 
Autostrade 
 
On the 23rd April Abertis, the listed Spanish motorway operator, agreed a merger with 
Autostrade. The terms of the deal involve Autostrade paying its shareholders a circa €4 
special dividend and then receiving Abertis stock in a merger. The exchange ratio was 
surprisingly generous to Abertis (10% to 15% worse than we would have negotiated) 
reflecting, we believe, a concern by the controlling shareholders of Autostrade to protect 
the company from future political risk through internationalising the company.  
Shareholders have approved the merger at an EGM, but it is still subject effectively to 
approval by the Italian government who are attempting to negotiate certain conditions 
on the deal. If the deal is approved we would expect the stock to rally, and likewise to 
fall if the deal is not approved. The Italian government has no legal jurisdiction over the 
deal and would effectively damage incentives for future investment by Autostrade if they 
reject the deal.

Arcelor and Arcelor Brazil 
 
During the quarter we made a significant investment in these two companies, with a 
weighting to the Brazilian stock. Arcelor is a European listed major steel producer with a 
Brazilian listed subsidiary generating over a third of its profits. The European investment 
was a bet that the hostile bid for Arcelor by Mittal Steel would be successful. Mittal 
raised its bid twice and Arcelor paid out a special dividend resulting in an attractive 
return on the Arcelor stock during the quarter. The Mittal deal has now been agreed by 
Arcelor.   
 
Arcelor Brazil, is one of the lowest cost steel producers in the world. Half its business is a 
duopoly with Gerdau in Brazil in long steel products – a truly excellent business where 
distribution is owned and controlled. It trades on approximately a 16% unleveraged 
normalized free cash flow yield in 2007 onwards and has substantial high return growth 
opportunities ($1.6bn of free cash flow on a $10bn market cap). Our actual free cash 
generation we expect in 2007 is nearer $2bn. Over time we would expect the company 
to be taken over by Mittal, although this may not be a priority for them in the short-
term. In any event, we think that the company will pay a high dividend in order to 
support the high leverage taken on by Mittal.    
 
Short Book 
 
The short book made a material contribution to the performance of the Fund this quarter 
generating returns of approximately 3% of NAV despite being in aggregate on average 
some 14% of NAV during the quarter including net short pairs.

7. Assets Under Management 
 
Post our Investor Day we concluded that the market risk was rising to a point where it 
made sense for us to reduce risk moderately in order to avoid being a forced seller if our 
portfolio declined another 10-15%. Risk reduction could be effected quickly by selling 
positions, shorting futures or taking capital. We concluded that because our portfolio was 
cheap and shorting futures created basis risk, that our investors were best served by 
offering them all a right to subscribe at NAV for 5% additional capital. We executed this 
on July 1 with some 85% of investors taking up these rights.

Over the last quarter, we have continued to build out the Operations and Finance teams

From an Operations perspective, we have successfully added two new officers to the

Another important hire made this quarter is Joseph O'Flynn. He is responsible for and
