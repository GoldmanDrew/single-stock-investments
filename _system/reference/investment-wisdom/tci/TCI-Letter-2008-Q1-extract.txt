3. Portfolio Review for Q1 2008 
 
 CSX 
 
CSX’s fourth quarter results were consistent with prior quarters: strong pricing, up 6.7% 
year-on-year, in spite of continued weak volumes, down 3%. CSX also repurchased 3% of 
its stock in the quarter and showed some signs of focus on productivity improvement. The 
net result was that for the full year in 2007, CSX comparable EPS increased 22% year-on-
year, despite a 3% decline in volumes.  
 
We expect volumes in 2008 to show continued weakness due to a soft US economy 
although our thesis is that pricing will remain firm and opportunities for further productivity 
gains remain.  
 
In March, the Company announced materially higher long-term guidance, increased its 
share repurchase program and raised its dividend. CSX is now targeting annual EPS growth 
before the impact of share repurchases of 18-21% through 2010 (previously 15-17% after 
share repurchases). The Board increased the size of the share repurchase program by $2.4 
billion; it now represents 13% of the Company’s market capitalisation and will be completed 
before the end of 2009. We believe the pressure applied by TCI is responsible for these 
recent actions.  
 
On the same day, CSX postponed its 2008 annual meeting and record date and filed a 
lawsuit against TCI and 3G Capital Partners alleging violations of federal securities laws. In 
response to this claim we issued the following statement: “It is unfortunate that CSX has 
chosen to manipulate the governance and Board election process by delaying the annual 
meeting and filing baseless claims. These allegations are wholly without merit and we will 
defend ourselves vigorously.” 
 
In April, TCI filed counterclaims against CSX and a third-party claim against its Chairman, 
Chief Executive Officer and President, Michael Ward, in the United States District Court – 
Southern District of New York, for violations of the federal securities laws and Virginia state 
law. Our claims against CSX and Mr. Ward allege that CSX failed to disclose, among other 
things, (i) that CSX awarded stock to its board and senior executives while in possession of 
material non-public information, (ii) the manner in which CSX's proposed bylaws would limit 
shareholders' right to call special meetings, and (iii) CSX's true intentions for commencing 
its litigation against TCI.

As we state in our counterclaims, “The lawsuit filed by CSX Corporation is the latest step in

The stock is up approximately 28% YTD. 
 
 Union Pacific 
 
The highlight of Union Pacific’s fourth quarter results was the Company’s strong focus on 
productivity and cost control. Pricing continued to be strong (+5-6% year-on-year), but 
management also succeeded in reducing costs 2% year-on-year despite flat volumes. 
Overall EPS only increased 4% due to abnormally harsh weather conditions and a lag-effect 
in recovering the exceptionally high fuel costs incurred during the quarter.  
 
For the full year in 2007, Union Pacific increased EPS by 17% year-on-year in spite of a 1% 
decline in volumes. The Company’s cost discipline was the best of all major North American 
railroads: operating costs per carload increased only 1% year-on-year versus 5% at CSX 
and 6% at Burlington Northern Sante Fe, its peer in the western US. 
 
As discussed in the CSX summary, continued weakness in the US economy will be a near-
term headwind for the US railroads. However, we believe that re-pricing of legacy contracts 
and continued productivity gains will allow Union Pacific to improve earnings significantly 
over the long term despite economic softness. 
 
 J-Power 
 
We have been stepping up our efforts to realise the intrinsic value of the company. In 
January, we applied to the Japanese government to increase our stake from 9.9% to 20% 
which is required under Japanese Foreign Exchange Law.  
 
In February, we wrote an open letter to the company asking them to introduce independent 
directors to monitor management decision making. In March, we followed up with 
submitting a 100-page plus detailed Business Plan demonstrating areas of focus for the 
company to improve corporate governance and value. 
 
At the same time, we significantly intensified our presence in local print and broadcast 
media to ensure our positive mindset, purpose and reputation are properly represented, 
with the message that our efforts are for the best interest of Japanese people and 
companies. This has led to a number of notable commentators and newspapers becoming 
openly supportive of our quest to increase shareholder value. We have plans to continue our 
persistent push on this front. 
 
The issue for this investment continues to be seeking a way for some of the massive cash 
flow generation of the company to be returned to shareholders rather than be 
predominately invested in value neutral investments.  The fact that foreign ownership is still 
at 40% leaves some potential that management will feel pressure to raise dividends by 
some amount in the next two months.

 Deutsche Boerse 
 
Deutsche Boerse had a very strong operating performance in the first quarter of the year. 
Eurex volumes have been on average 26% above the same period last year and 21% above 
the average of 2007. In addition the mix of volume is more geared to futures contracts 
(mainly equity contracts) which attract a higher margin for the group. ISE volumes are also 
benefiting from the increase in volatility, achieving an amazing 50% growth in average daily 
volume in Q1 2008 over Q1 2007. For Q1, transaction volume in Xetra was up over 50%. 
 
Clearstream continues to grow nicely in custody with 6% growth in assets under custody 
versus last year. Its new initiative with Eurex, Global Cash management (a collateral 
management business), is growing very strongly at above 30% and now accounts for close 
to 30% of Clearstream revenues. The settlement activities have been mixed with two strong 
months in January and March and a weak February.   
 
In January, Deutsche Boerse announced an unexpected reduction of its tax rate from 32% 
to 25% within 3 years as the company moved half of its employees outside Frankfurt. This 
is further evidence of the strong alignment we believe management and shareholders now 
have. 
 
In spite of this solid operating performance, Deutsche Boerse shares have been seriously 
de-rated during the quarter falling 25%. Several factors can explain this poor performance. 
First the shares have been penalised by the poor performance of the German market which 
fell around 20% during the quarter. Second, there has been profit taking after a very strong 
performance during 2007 when the shares doubled. In addition, CME shares (one of its 
closest comparable companies) de-rated after the department of Justice sent a letter asking 
the US treasury to look into the vertically integrated structure of the industry and the 
Treasury suggested to merge the CFTC and the SEC in a published blueprint. Our due 
diligence with various industry players and regulators lead us to believe that the chances of 
a change in CME regulatory framework are remote.   
 
 Indian Banks 
 
Indian banks in last quarter have been a tale of two stories. At the company level, all banks 
have reported very strong and better than expected earnings for the last quarter (ranging 
from EPS growth of 25% to 70% year-on-year). The main features have been improving net 
interest margins (the benefit of the hikes in loan rates), continuous improvement in asset 
quality (NPLs formation dropped from 3.7% 2002 to 1.5% in 2007) and robust credit 
growth (25% on average). 
 
At the political level, the performance of PSU banks has been affected by the government 
initiative to cancel the non performing loans for the small farmers for a total amount of 
600bn Rupees. PSU banks account for around 25% of the loans. After a period of 
uncertainty, the Minister of Finance has announced that all banks will be fully compensated 
in cash for the gross value of the loan (we estimate that more than a third of these loans 
are currently non performing). Banks will therefore write back the provisions they had 
made. By the completion of the programme in three years, the PSU banks will see around 
14% increase in their book value.

Even if the Indian government has never displayed any signs of abusing minority investors, 
the government intervention has increased the perceived risk and the risk of moral hazard 
on lending. Some large brokers have downgraded the sector on the back of an increase risk 
premium and rising provisions on their agriculture business. 
 
The second factor which has affected the Indian banks is the change of market expectation 
regarding interest rates. The market was betting on a cut in interest rates at the start of the 
year, given the low level of inflation in India and the risk of a slowdown in the economy 
driven by the slowdown in the US. In February, banks have cut their prime lending rate 
from 13.3% to 12.8% in anticipation of lower funding costs. After bottoming at 3.4%, 
inflation has rebounded strongly to reach 7% last week. The increase in inflation has been 
driven by increase in metal and food prices. This uncertainty on monetary policy has created 
some fears that Indian banks margins could be affected by an increase in the Indian central 
banks bank reserve requirements – cash held with no interest with the central bank (CRR).  
 
Although it is not possible to call the course of Indian monetary policy, we do not share the 
market concerns on margins in the medium term. The managements of all PSU banks have 
been very focused on margins for several years. If the central bank increases its cash 
balance with the banks, it is very likely that banks will lower the deposits costs (a lot of high 
cost term deposits contracted last year are due for renewal in this year) and slow their 
volume growth to protect their margins. The managements of Union Bank and PNB have 
already publicly stated that they will focus on margins over volume in 2008. Within one or 
two quarters, banks will have the possibility to compensate for some margin loss. In 
addition, the liquidity is very abundant in India this year with deposits growing in line or 
above loans, so banks will be able to grow their low cost deposits, avoiding raising 
expensive term deposits. 
 
A potential slowdown of volume growth from 25% to 20% is in line with our expectations 
and will bring banks to a more sustainable level of growth. Given their current RoE of 
around 20%, Indian banks are not able to sustain a growth rate above 20% without raising 
fresh equity. In addition, banks can increase their deposits in line with loans, allowing them 
to increase their margins. 
 
After the recent substantial market sell off in India, PSU banks trade between 5x and 7x 
March 2009 earnings and 0.9x to 1.3x P/BV (OBC being at the low end and SBI at the high 
end of the range). The sector will continue to display around 20-30% underlying EPS growth 
for the foreseeable future with RoE stabilising around 20%. 
 
 European Bank Investments 
 
Our current investments in European banks comprise approximately 5% long and 1% short.  
The core positions are Intesa-SanPaolo, Credit Suisse and Garanti Bank in Turkey. Overall 
this quarter our losses in banks were limited by hedges that we initiated early in the year.  
It is our view that wholesale funded banks will have margins squeezed but fully deposit 
funded banks like Intesa will have strong margin expansion and volume growth due to 
weakened competitors. 
 
4. Outlook for 2008 
 
The portfolio was impacted negatively by the broad based falls in equity markets around the 
world and moved down in line with equity markets. We have often made it clear to investors 
that we are always exposed to significant market risk due to our long biased strategy and 
hope to offset any negative beta over time with significant alpha generation.

The causes of the market falls (credit crisis in US and Europe and US recession) are well 
documented. The fund, in anticipation of market falls, executed some material equity index 
and stock specific shorts in Asia and the US which we subsequently covered generating 
gains of around 3% of the fund NAV.  Our major mistake on this was not executing any 
material short exposure in Europe which fell more violently than the US.  At their worst 
European markets were down 20%. The continual strengthening of the Euro helped 
contribute to this. 
 
Our view is that the portfolio is cheap today and full of very high quality dominant 
businesses with potential to compound at 15% long-term. This belief resulted in CIFF 
investing a further $400m into the fund on April 1.   
 
Cash levels are high and interest rates low. Credit Suisse estimates that total money market 
funds divided by the S&P 500 Market Cap equals 28% today. The last time this ratio was 
this high was July 1982 and February 2003 two of the best buying opportunities in market 
history. 
 
There is no doubt that the US and Europe economies will be challenged by deleveraging of 
consumers and financial services companies and economic growth will be subdued in these 
regions. Emerging market economic growth we believe will remain strong. It is entirely 
possible that the current rally that the market is enjoying in April fails and we retest market 
lows. 
 
Our approach is to remain committed to the long term fully invested strategy that we have 
adopted since the beginning of the fund. Corporate values are driven by a 20 year not 2  
year horizon and we are willing to take equity market volatility in return for long-term 
capital appreciation.

6. Counterparty Risk 
 
We have been very focused, for the last year, on reducing the fund’s counterparty risk due 
to our long standing concerns with the credit of broker dealers. As per the supplement to 
the offering memoranda circulated to all investors in December 2007, the fund has entered 
into a custody agreement with HSBC which gives us full ownership of securities custodied 
there with the full right of return of these securities. HSBC also guarantees performance of 
any sub-custodians used. Today the majority of our fund assets are custodied with HSBC 
under this agreement.   
 
The remaining equity securities are nearly all held in segregated accounts in the fund’s 
name with a variety of major broker dealers. Re-hypothecation (securities lending) is only 
permitted to the level of indebtedness to our prime brokers. We regularly monitor 
compliance of the broker dealers with the segregation account status.  Nearly all cash of the 
fund is held today within commercial banks.  
 
Except under exceptional circumstances we do not use pooled accounts and today have 
under $1m held in pooled accounts – the standard form in which hedge funds hold assets 
with prime brokers. 
 
As a result, absent fraud of counterparties, we feel comfortable that we have strongly 
protected the fund against failure of one or all broker dealers with whom we do business.
