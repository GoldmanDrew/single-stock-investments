3. Portfolio Review for Q2 2008 
 
During Q2 we suffered major losses in our European and Indian positions offset by gains in 
our US book.   
 
Deutsche Boerse 
 
The biggest detractor was Deutsche Boerse which declined by a third during the quarter.  
Exchanges globally have suffered a violent derating this year as investors fear that trading 
activity will dry up and values of stocks traded decline. For the cash market these concerns 
are valid. For derivative exchanges there is no slowdown at all in Eurex in Q2, with volumes 
running at over 9m contracts per day on average similarly to its very strong Q1 and up over 
15% year on year. Clearstream is still growing year on year we believe despite weak 
issuance of bonds.   
 
It is our belief that in 2009 Deutsche Boerse will earn over 6 euros per share of free cash 
flow and is currently at a 9% free cash flow yield on these estimates. To us this prices no 
growth for the whole business ever. Even if you assume only 5% long term growth this 
would offer 50% upside to the stock. The company has started a 400m euro share buyback 
at the start of July. One important growth driver we highlighted at our investor day is the 
clearing of OTC derivatives for investment banks. We have clear evidence from interviewing 
some of these banks that there is a strong desire near term to move significant clearing 
volumes to Eurex. 
 
Indian Banks 
 
Our Indian banks took a further massive fall and are down 30-50% since the start of the 
year. The market is concerned that inflation and higher central bank rates to tackle this will 
shrink net interest margins and increase non-performing loans and reduce credit growth.  
Our view is that these impacts will be limited and banks have raised their lending margins 
to maintain their spreads. Punjab National Bank’s chairman publicly reiterated that they 
would grow earnings 20% this year. The stock trades on 5x our earnings estimate for the 
current year, similar to the valuation of all our Indian Bank holdings. 
 
High oil prices, 12% inflation and political uncertainty are scaring investors out of equities in 
India. The equity market there is down over 30% year to date. We do not believe that 
growth will stop in India although it may certainly slow.

CSX 
 
The AGM of CSX took place on June 25th. We believe, based on our preliminary estimates, 
that we succeeded in winning 4 board seats of the 5 which we had sought for our nominees.  
The company will not release official results until July 25th. The high oil price, which gets 
fully passed through with a short lag generally by the rail companies, increases their already 
high competitive price advantage versus trucks. 
 
Mastercard and Visa 
 
During Q2 Mastercard announced new long term guidance of EPS growth of 20-30% per 
annum. Our holding in Visa appreciated strongly during the quarter (doubling since the IPO 
price) and we chose to move some of this capital into our Mastercard holding and eliminate 
the rest of the position for now. 
 
4. Outlook for 2008 
 
The outlook for equity markets looks bleak. Economies are slowing everywhere as the high 
oil price, US consumer recession, weakened banks and high inflation sap consumer 
confidence and spending. European equity markets have now fallen around 25% this year 
and show no sign of stopping their fall.   
 
We think that our companies remain strong but they will all have some economic exposure 
and cannot escape a major recession and continued equity market slump. We are focussed 
on finding shorts on consumer spending which we think will be seriously challenged by the 
current environment and limiting on leverage.

6. Counterparty Risk 
 
Actions taken by TCI over Q2 2008 
 
TCI has continued to focus on managing its counterparty risk during the recent challenges in 
the markets. The value of assets in custody with HSBC has increased from 51% in March to 
68% at June-end and this is expected to further increase. We have also eliminated exposure 
to “pooled accounts” at prime brokers and currently 85% of the fund’s NAV is in custody or 
segregated accounts. With the exception of HSBC and clearing banks, TCI believe the fund’s 
NAV loss in all our broker dealers being bankrupt at once would be less than 5%, 
representing cash and swap profit at such counterparties and excluding the value of assets 
in segregated accounts.
