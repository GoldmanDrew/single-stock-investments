3. Portfolio Review for Q4 2008 
 
CSX and Union Pacific 
 
Our position in CSX has been materially reduced at the end of the quarter to approximately 
11% of the fund as a result of the expiration of one of our two swap positions.  Our position 
in Union Pacific was also reduced and now represents approximately 2% of the fund.  
 
Rail volumes during November and December have shown significant weakness as a result 
of large declines in industrial production, housing starts and automotive production. A 
material part of this is due to destocking which will, at some, point reverse. Our core thesis 
on continued pricing power and cost reductions driving long term earnings growth remains 
intact but it is likely that 2009 will not be a year of earnings growth for railway companies if 
the economy remains as weak as it currently is. In our view, the massive declines in stock 
prices during the quarter have left these stocks significantly undervalued.

the need for Deutsche Boerse to consider a merger and we believe that there are essentially

Indian Banks 
 
There were a number of positive developments for our Indian bank holdings during the 
quarter. The most important of these was multiple interest rate cuts from the central bank 
of India which has driven down bond yields to close to 5%. This has created significant book 
value gains for all of the public sector banks we own because of their very large holdings of 
government bonds. 
 
The Indian Central Bank has also cut, to around 5% of assets, the cash that must be held 
by banks with the central bank itself. This frees up more capital for banks to lend at 
profitable rates. Despite an expected increase in non-performing loan provisions to between 
1% and 2% of loans we still expect earnings to grow 20% pa as top line of 15-20% growth 
and cost growth of 10% creates operating leverage despite higher provisioning. 
 
It is worth noting that India never had anything close to the problems experienced 
elsewhere for the subprime loan, securitisations or home equity loans (except for Citibank 
India). Part of the credit for this goes to a strong regulator, V.Y. Reddy, who was the 
governor of the Reserve Bank of India. He imposed strict lending standards including 
banning lending against raw land except for construction loans. Banks in India were banned 
from securitising loans so they had an incentive to make sure lending standards were 
maintained. Risk weightings on commercial real estate lending were also increased. As a 
result Indian public sector banks have so far not had any material problems from the 
financial crisis and are fully deposit-funded institutions with strong growth in their deposits. 
Loan to deposit ratios are now approaching 100% and the loan book is now only growing in 
line with deposit growth. 
 
We do believe that these stocks can perform well during 2009 due to their extremely low 
multiples and continued earnings growth in a falling interest rate environment.

Visa 
 
During the quarter we eliminated the fund’s Mastercard position and concentrated our card 
processor investment in Visa. We believe Visa is a stronger company due to its far superior 
position in the US debit cards business, where it has a 70% market share in this structurally 
growing segment. In addition, the creditworthiness of its credit card users is materially 
superior to that of Mastercard giving likely outperformance in volumes. Moreover, we think 
that Visa will meet earnings expectations in 2009 due to strong cost cutting and price rises 
even if volume growth overall is mid-single digit.   
 
The issue of overall spending volumes is a concern on our investment in Visa. People will 
clearly spend less due to the recession. We have tried to analyse the impact of this by 
reviewing long periods of historic data going back 80 years. Personal Consumption 
Expenditure (PCE) has tended to be not worse and mostly better than nominal GDP in bad 
economic times. This is logical because capex is more volatile than consumption. The worst 
nominal consumption, besides the great depression, has been -2%. We forecast a -2% 
nominal volume growth for Visa in the US and a -5% for PCE in the economy which is 
consistent with a real and nominal GDP of -5% in 2009 (far worse than consensus 
expectations). PCE in October was +2% and +0.5% in November.  
 
In summary the valuation case remains that normal (non-recession) top line of 7-8% ex 
pricing due to plastic penetration and PCE growth should translate into 12% EPS growth due 
to operating leverage plus the 6% entry free cash yield should give 15-20% total 
shareholder returns. 
 
4. Outlook 
 
Risks to financial markets and the economy remain very high. Banks are only just beginning 
a multi-year period of deleveraging, which will reduce credit in the real economy 
significantly. It is our opinion that the problems in banks are materially worse than is 
generally assumed and deteriorated significantly in November and December. We believe 
that many European banks would be insolvent if they marked their assets down to today’s 
fair level. The US government rescue of Citigroup suggests that US banks have similar 
problems. 
 
Some of the best real-time indicators show clearly that the US economy is in free fall.  
Income tax withholdings fell nearly 6% in December due to lower bonuses, job losses and 
temporary plant closings. Job postings have tumbled double digit in November and 
December, suggesting the US economy is shedding jobs at a huge rate. To some degree 
offsetting this are the lowest mortgage rates in 40 years and collapsing oil prices, benefiting 
the struggling US consumer. The big picture problem remains that the US consumer, a key 
driver of global growth, will spend less because of the lack of readily available credit, a fall 
in wealth because of the fall in house prices and stock markets, and the decrease in job 
security. 
  
The bubble in credit markets pushed economies and corporate earnings materially above 
their normal levels in all sectors of the economy. Estimating where earnings will settle for 
many sectors is very challenging today.

7. CIFF 
 
As you are aware, The Children’s Investment Fund Foundation (UK) (CIFF) is a significant 
investor in the fund and has held substantially all of its capital in the fund since TCI’s 
inception. As a result of recent events in global financial markets and CIFF’s statutory duty 
of care in relation to the exercise of its investment powers, the board of CIFF has decided to 
diversify its investment portfolio. During November 2008 CIFF requested a redemption of 
45% of its holdings in the fund. The directors of the fund approved the redemption, which 
was met from the available cash reserves of the fund. The fund continues to hold significant 
cash holdings. 
 
CIFF has also requested a transfer of its remaining interest in the fund to a managed 
account probably at the end of Q1 2009. CIFF (US), the US sister foundation to CIFF, is also 
an investor in TCI, (albeit much smaller) and has also requested a redemption of its 
holdings in the fund to be effected at the time of the transfer of CIFF’s interest to the 
managed account. In order to effect the transfer and the redemption, the fund will 
aggregate the holdings of CIFF and CIFF US and will then transfer their pro rata share of the 
underlying assets held by the fund (including cash).  CIFF (US) will receive only cash and 
CIFF will receive a slightly higher proportion of assets with less cash. In aggregate CIFF 
(US) and CIFF will not receive more than their aggregate pro-rata entitlement to cash or

9. Investor Relations 
 
Liquidity profile 
 
With lock-ups of three and five years, the fund has capital committed to it until 2013. Unlike 
many of our competitors, the lock-ups end on a monthly basis and the fund does not have 
the issue of having to deal with quarterly or annual cliffs. All external client redemptions 
made in 2008 were replaced with new capital. At this stage there are minor redemptions 
that have been placed for 2009 amounting to 4% of the fund’s total assets. Given the 
turbulent market conditions and our large asset base, we are happy to let these small 
redemptions go without being replaced. The situation is being constantly reviewed.  
 
Gates and redemption suspensions 
 
With widespread news on funds putting up gates and suspending redemptions, the portfolio 
is sufficiently liquid to meet any redemptions that might occur. Moreover, it should be noted 
that investors who redeem will typically continue to receive their monies 15-20 days into 
the month following the finalisation of the NAV.

Appendix 2 – The Children’s Investment Fund Foundation (CIFF) 2008 Summary  
 
Most significantly in 2008, CIFF Trustees raised the bar on the Foundation’s aspirations for 
its charitable programs. This decision was based on CIFF’s growth in assets coupled with a 
growing track record and confidence in its ability to effect change. In place of seeking 
national change around a set of specific issues, CIFF is now committed to funding 
programs with the potential for global transformation in seven areas that span the life 
cycle of children: child survival, hunger and nutrition, early childhood development, 
educational attainment, the care environment, reproductive health and economic 
readiness.   
 
Seeking “Big Win” Investments 
 
CIFF aims to invest consistently behind programs with potential for “big wins” — i.e. 
opportunities to effect a profound improvement in well-being indicators in our priority 
impact areas benefiting large numbers of children, and where success extends well beyond 
the direct impact of the funded program. Our investment criteria remain unchanged from 
CIFF’s historical requirements: tangible targets for impacting children, scale, replicability, 
sustainability, value for money and leverage. Recent examples of how this investment 
philosophy is translating into specific investments include: backing a low-cost 
methodology that has the potential to help millions of children to read and do basic math 
(who would otherwise have grown up unable to do so); funding a cohort of partners in a 
coordinated effort whose ultimate objective is the virtual elimination of pediatric 
HIV/AIDS; and saving over 50,000 children’s lives annually through a global initiative to 
reach one billion people with essential nutrients by fortifying staple foods. 
 
AID INDIA & Educational Attainment 
 
Although 95% of India’s children attend primary school, fewer than half of those who 
reach the equivalent of 5th grade can read at a basic level. Currently the norm is for 
teachers in India (and elsewhere in the developing world) to teach hugely overcrowded 
classes through rote learning. CIFF is providing the funding to expand AID India’s 
approach to pedagogy in Tamil Nadu, which is among the bottom three states in reading 
and math (65% of 5th graders cannot read at a second grade level and 83% cannot do 
simple division). AID India trains and supports teachers in the state school system to 
classify and group children according to their ability levels in a growing range of subjects. 
They then introduce teachers to experiential learning and provide them with materials to 
enable children to progress through the stages of competency. In a “pilot” of 450,000 
children in 7,300 schools, independent research demonstrated that that AID India’s 
approach increased the number of children able to read from 54% to 85% in 5 months, all 
at an annual cost of little more than $1 per child. 
 
Currently, CIFF is enabling AID India to expand to firmly establish the pedagogy in 1/3 of 
Tamil Nadu’s districts and fund an external validation of impact. If the model proves 
successful, it is understood that the Tamil Nadu government will adopt the pedagogy 
state-wide, incorporating all of its 10 million primary students into this learning approach.  
CIFF anticipates that proven success in Tamil Nadu will inform India’s national education 
agenda. We are also exploring opportunities to adapt and expand aspects of the AID India 
methodology into Africa. 
 
Prevention of Mother to Child Transmission & Child Survival 
 
AIDS continues to be the leading cause of mortality in Africa, contributing to the deaths of 
approximately 300,000 children under the age of 15 annually. Virtually all of these 
children contracted the disease either in pregnancy, birth or through breastfeeding.  
Through a partnership with the Clinton Foundation, CIFF has enabled affordable treatment 
of infected children at scale. Yet, the imperative is to prevent children contracting the

agencies and other practitioners) into adopting this more effective intervention as the 
norm. 
 
GAIN & Hunger and Nutrition 
 
Deficiencies of four nutrients—vitamin A, iron, folic acid and zinc—are proven to be among 
the key contributors to maternal and child mortality, pre-mature births, stunting, diarrhea, 
pneumonia, malaria, blindness and neural tube defects. In partnership with the Gates 
Foundation, CIFF has funded the Global Alliance for Improved Nutrition (GAIN) to fortify 
the most prevalent household food items in 22 countries, ranging from rice to fish sauce 
to cornmeal, with these minerals and vitamins. Among the strengths of the initiative is 
that the health of 1 billion people can be improved without requiring any behavior change 
and with negligible ongoing costs to consumers, producers and governments. 
 
GAIN has been consistently successful in getting producers to voluntarily begin fortifying 
their products, and in supporting them (in partnership with the World Health Organisation) 
by directing the amounts and constitution of nutrients to be added to specific items and 
certifying the enhanced product quality. In addition to technical support, GAIN contributes 
to initial costs of purchasing mixers, quality control oversight, and public awareness 
campaigns as well as negotiating with vitamin suppliers and supporting a loan mechanism 
for the initial purchase of the nutrient mixes for smaller producers.   
 
Central to the success of the initiative is the buy-in and cooperation between government 
and the private sector. GAIN thus facilitates the development of high functioning national 
fortification alliances that include the relevant ministerial posts, producers and other key 
stakeholders. The program ultimately requires strong policy mandates for fortification and 
on-going supervision.   
 
In several target countries, GAIN will also pilot initiatives to reach poorer, and the most 
at-risk populations, for example by working with producers to sell items in smaller 
quantities and ensuring the World Food Program is distributing fortified products.  A robust 
system is being put in place to measure biometric indicators, as well as direct changes in 
health outcomes. At a minimum, it is anticipated that this initiative will result within 5 
years in the annual aversion of 55,000 child deaths and 3,500 maternal deaths. 
 
Foundation Staffing 
 
We have this past year recruited heavily and succeeded in bringing aboard most of the 
staff required to deliver CIFF’s more ambitious aspirations. Located in hubs in London, 
Delhi and Nairobi, the team now numbers 35, double the level from a year ago. CIFF 
operates along the lines of a private equity model. Staff are divided into an “investment 
team” charged with identifying the organisations and ideas that can deliver 
transformational change for children and a “portfolio team” that ensures that programs
