3. Portfolio Review for Q3 2009 
 
Some of our core positions are outlined below in summary form. 
 
Visa 
 
Visa is up 11% in the quarter.  It is our largest holding, currently representing 10% of 
the fund’s size.  
 
Operating performance in the quarter continued to be strong, in line with expectations 
against a very tough consumer backdrop, and the most challenging quarterly comparison 
period of the year.  Transaction growth was steady at above 8%, payment volume 
growth at 2% and operating earnings grew 18%.  We expect all metrics to reaccelerate 
heading into the fourth fiscal quarter and the coming financial year, on the back of new 
pricing initiatives and a slightly more benign consumer spending environment. 
 
News’ flow continues on potential changes to the regulatory environment, which can 
make the Visa share-price somewhat volatile.  We continue to believe that the Visa 
economic model will prove resilient, even to some of the most prejudicial regulatory 
outcomes for interchange.

Visa is a very small share of system economics, it does not earn interchange, its value to 
the system is extremely difficult to replicate, and the precedents from other markets is 
that the consumer typically directly picks up the cost of a regulatory intervention and the 
merchants pick up extra margin.  
 
On the 8th October the US House of Representatives Financial Services Committee held a 
hearing on interchange fees.  Representative Luis Gutierrez (D-Illinois) was very critical 
of interchange fees, but other Democrats came to the defense of the industry, including 
Brad Sherman (D-California) and Mel Watt (D-North Carolina), who sit on both Financial 
Services and Judiciary committees.  Both criticized merchants, who are likely to keep 
any decrease in interchange fees without passing the benefit on to consumers.  It would 
result in consumers having to pay more for a card, due to higher fees from banks.  In 
addition, House Financial Services Chairman Barney Frank left after 20 minutes after 
giving his opening comment.  Reporters pressed him in the hallway on the issue but 
Frank gave no indication that he intended to move legislation. 
 
The next step on US political scrutiny on interchange is possible in the Senate. Senate 
Banking Chairman Chris Dodd (D-Connecticut) has discussed bringing up interchange 
legislation.  Our research suggests he is not ready to bring a bill but a hearing is 
certainly feasible. 
 
Australia is the most commonly cited precedent of regulatory intervention. There, the 
consumer banking market is more consolidated than in the US, yet a considerably more 
dramatic intervention in interchange than is being currently considered in the US 
resulted in no change to Visa or MasterCard’s economic model.  
 
On our current estimates, and excluding the group’s cash position, Visa trades on 15x 
2010 calendar year earnings, a substantial discount to what we perceive to be a fair 
trading multiple, given the quality and structural growth outlook for the business.   
 
BAT 
 
BAT increased by over 17% in GBP during the third quarter. 
 
Following our initiation of a position in British American Tobacco (BAT) during Q2 2009, 
we now have a 8.3% position of NAV in the world’s second largest cigarette company, 
with a leadership position in more than 50 markets worldwide. 
 
With 77% of volumes and 60% of profits coming from emerging markets, BAT is best 
positioned in the global tobacco industry to profit from the premiumisation of emerging 
market consumers.  We therefore believe its long-term growth model underwriting 6% 
EBIT growth remains intact and at 11.5x our forward earnings estimate, close to 9% 
levered free cash flow yield and less than 2x Net Debt/EBITDA, we would expect to 
generate a low risk, high-teens long-term shareholder return. 
 
The company held an investor day in Brazil in September, which we attended.  In 
addition, we have met the management in London in September.  Key points from these 
meetings were as follows. 
 
Although management expects some near-term volume weakness in the industry (1-
2%) due to rising unemployment around the world, it will continue to deliver on its long-
term growth model by taking real pricing.   
 
The company is targeting 4% revenue growth, driven by pricing.  It is worth 
remembering that, due to the fact that excise taxes are a significant component of the 
end consumer price, modest increases in retail price produce much larger increases in 
the manufacturers’ take.

We would expect this revenue growth to lead to a double digit operating profit growth, 
especially combined with extracting the second half of an £800m cost cutting 
programme.  However, management target a 6% annual operating profit growth, which 
we think is too conservative. Operating margins are targeted to increase from 30% to

The key point is that the industry has strong pricing power due to the consolidation and 
shared strategy of the industry and also low pricing relative to purchasing power in 
emerging markets.  We met with the management teams of PMI and Japan Tobacco this 
quarter; they are all extremely aggressive in structural pricing targets for their 
businesses.  Excise tax increases thus far in 2009 have been relatively benign, despite 
outliers in the Ukraine and Brazil.  Minimum taxes often help to minimise the impact of 
down trading, which is a key risk in all consumer products companies today. 
 
Other points emphasised is the significant geographic diversification of their portfolio in 
50 countries and their consistent increase in market share. Their global drive brands, 
representing only 33% of their mix, were growing at double digit rates. 
 
The management emphasised in our meeting that their 65% payout ratio representing a 
nearly 6% forward dividend yield is not a floor and could well be increased.  With 
earnings long term being all free cash flow, this underpins the total shareholder return. 
 
E.ON 
 
E.ON is a 4.5% position for the fund. 
 
E.ON is one of Europe’s largest power generation utilities with significant regulated 
distribution assets and a strong gas pipe-line and supply business.  The value case for 
E.ON remains strong, despite current tough trading conditions and the rebound in the 
share price.  We believe the company can sustain €3.0 of earnings per share from 2010, 
putting it on 9x earnings, whereas a fair multiple, in our opinion, would be closer to 11x.  
Moreover, our earnings number is based on a long-term USD oil price in the mid 50s 
which, given that every $10 increase in the oil price adds 10-15% to fair value, provides 
us with additional optionality.  For example, oil at $100/bbl and other commodities, such 
as coal and carbon, in line with this and fully reflected in power prices, could imply E.ON 
fair value closer to €50/share and 80% upside. 
 
The recent centre-right victory in the German elections is positive for German utilities.  A 
pro-growth economic agenda should help reverse the energy demand destruction seen in 
2009.  In addition, Chancellor Merkel is supportive of lifetime extensions (15 years) to 
the German nuclear power fleet.  While we expect any benefits to be shared, nuclear life 
extensions could add 10% to our E.ON fair value. 
 
We attended the recent Capital Markets day for E.ON in Germany. Key points made by 
management, including the new CEO, are that they expect their €15bn investment 
program to add €1.2bn of EBIT by 2015, based on the current spreads implied by power 
markets.  E.ON’s view is that the power market will remain soggy, due to recent demand 
reductions, but that they believed that by 2015 they would be tight again.  Overall there 
is a story of self help of cost cutting, disposals and organic growth remain intact.   
 
By 2012 the company will be on at least a 12% free cash flow yield under conservative 
oil price assumptions.  The dividend yield remains in excess of 5%.

IFN’s revenues are 80% long-term contracted, EBITDA margins are 70%+ and assets 
are financed under a global debt facility expiring in 2022.  We initiated the position in

The M&A market for contracted operating wind assets remains strong, with recent 
achieved prices in-line with those seen in early 2008.  This and the desire to focus on the 
fast-growing Australian business, led to IFN’s recent decision to sell its residual European 
assets and the US portfolio.   For example, in the US, B&B has just sold its contracted 
wind assets to Florida Power & Light for $352m, an implied capacity multiple of 
$1903/kW for the 185MW.  In Europe, recent sales suggest 15-20% premia to new build 
cost of €1.3/kW.  In Australia, AGL has just sold a 132MW uncompleted windfarm in 
South Australia for an implied A$3015/kW to the Energy Infrastructure Investments 
consortia (Marubeni, APA Group, Osaka Gas).  IFN has excellent growth potential in 
Australia.  We estimate a potential doubling of operating assets and EBITDA over the 
next 3 yrs through the development of its 1,000MW pipeline.   The recently strengthened 
federal renewable energy targets (RET) and the likelihood of agreement on a carbon 
abatement regime later this year/early next year provide a strong industrial backdrop for 
this growth.  
 
Successful execution of IFN’s asset sales (targeted for Q4 2009/Q1 2010) in line with 
these sale multiples and a peer-average rating for IFN’s Australian business, would imply 
an equity value for IFN of A$2.3-2.5/share - further upside of circa 60%.   
 
Lockheed Martin 
 
Lockheed Martin is approximately a 4.5% position for the fund. 
 
Lockheed Martin is the largest and best defence company in the world and the majority 
of the business is monopolistic.  We think it can be a good investment because it is 
cheap (12% free cash flow yield ex-pension cash flows, which we believe will be 
remunerated), will grow free cash flow at 5% p.a. for the next 5 years due to the F-35 
program, management are shareholder friendly, margins and competitive position are 
structurally protected by their strong proprietary technology, it is the choice of the 
Pentagon for sole supplier status in many programs which embed monopolistic positions 
and it has a broad range of high value added complex programs. 
 
The business is split into four divisions.  The largest is Aeronautics (31% profit).  They 
make both of the US fifth generation fighters (the F-35 Lightning and the F-22 Raptor) 
and provide most of the Western world’s military heavy lift capacity with the C-130 
Hercules and C-5 Super Galaxy. 
 
Electronics (30% profit) encompasses a range of missile, radar, electronic warfare and 
naval systems.  Missile defence is the biggest single area for this division.  
 
They manufacture the Aegis system for the navy and the land-based interceptors THAAD 
and PAC-3 (in collaboration with Raytheon).  Lockheed was not involved in the recently 
cancelled missile shield and in fact this decision and re-prioritisation has helped their 
business. 
 
Information Systems and Global Services (21% profit) provides software and system 
integration to the military, intelligence and federal government.  The business is highly 
diversified with the largest contract accounting for 3% of the division’s revenues.  
Contracts include managing air traffic control centres, developing biometric security for 
the government and command and control systems.

Space is the smallest division and accounts for 18% of profits.  The largest programs are 
classified which obviously presents hurdles to detailed analysis, but from the checks that 
we have made we think funding for these programs is robust. 
 
The defence sector is out of favour (trading at a 0.6 relative EV/EBIT multiple versus a 
historic range of 0.6x-1.0x) as investors are concerned about tougher defence budgets 
under President Obama.  US defence spending is as high as it was at the height of the 
Cold War in constant dollars but not relative to GDP (4% GDP currently compared to 6% 
in the Cold War).   
 
We think that spending will fall as troops are withdrawn from Iraq and defence 
supplementals are cut, but we forecast that procurement (the part Lockheed's sales 
come from) in the base budget will remain constant in real terms despite the deficit as 
the US has to replace old equipment.  In 1987 the average age of USAF aircraft was 15 
years, it is now 26 years and we are starting to see operational losses from metal 
fatigue.  Additionally, the US has fought a long war in harsh conditions in the Gulf and 
Afghanistan where most of their equipment has been operating at 2-5x peacetime 
intensity, which has considerably shortened service lives. 
 
Investors are also cautious about the large, $12bn (40% of the market cap), stated 
pension deficit at Lockheed.  The accounting behind this is esoteric and complicated, but 
our work supports management’s assertion that this deficit will be remunerated by the 
federal government, although there will be distortions in both the earnings and cash 
flow. 
 
Lockheed should continue to grow for at least the next decade as the F-35, their largest 
program, ramps up.  EBIT from this program should grow from $250m in ’09 to $1.7bn 
in ’16- meaningful given total EBIT ’09 of $4.4bn.  The plane is high priority to replace 
some 9,000 ageing and high maintenance fighters and ground attack aircraft (our 
checks here have been consistent that this program will continue to be well supported 
even if budgets come under great pressure).  Outside of Aeronautics, their outlook is 
program specific.  We think that the missile defence business (THAAD, Aegis, PAC-3, 
SIBIRS) will continue to grow as missile capabilities of North Korea and Iran advance, 
the Orion NASA program has firm funding while parts of the systems and services 
business will see cuts.  Overall, we forecast the business ex-aeronautics to be flat.   
 
The company has strong visibility with a firm order-book of $80bn (2x annual sales) and 
should grow earnings at 5% CAGR ’09-’16.  Valuation is attractive, trading on 9.3x ’09 
pension adjusted earnings (6.7x EV/EBIT ’09) and is very cash generative (12% ’09 
underlying FCF yield rising to close to 15% in ’16) with a strong record of returning cash 
to shareholders.   The company pays a 3% dividend yield and excluding limited 
acquisitions all free cash flow is used for buybacks which have been $2bn pa for the last 
3 years on a $29bn market cap.  A 14-15% 5 year IRR is feasible.

SAP is currently a 5% position for the fund. 
 
A private two hour meeting with SAP's CEO and CFO last month at SAP headquarters in 
Germany has helped to reaffirm our confidence in the quality of SAP's business and the

We believe SAP will keep earnings flat this year before delivering 15-20% p.a. earnings 
growth through 2012. Our estimates build in strong cost management and partial 
implementation of previously announced price increases but assume a relatively subdued 
cyclical recovery.

Our conviction that SAP will meet or beat our numbers has increased post our meeting 
for the following reasons.  
 
(i) 
Management remains committed to increasing margins from 25% to 35% over the 
next few years. As a benchmark, its competitor Oracle has 48% operating margins; 
 
(ii) 
Maintenance price increases (from 17% of sales to 22% phased in over 6 years) 
are being successfully and broadly implemented; 
 
(iii) 
Management believes it has significant growth opportunities both with existing and 
new customers.  Specifically they believe they can double the volume of business 
with existing customers over the long term via innovation and broadening the user 
base within their clients.  Regarding new customers, management highlighted 
emerging markets, which currently represent less than 10% of sales, and the 
$15bn mid-market segment which will be targeted by the company's new software-
as-a-service offering; 
 
(iv) Customer retention was confirmed as close to 99% with customers only leaving in 
the event of bankruptcy or mergers; 
 
(v) 
The acquisition of Business Objects has been well received by customers and is 
operating well. 
 
Regarding the quality of SAP as a business, the key take-away was the level of ongoing 
innovation and ever-increasing sophistication of SAP's product.  We believe this 
innovation increases SAP's lock over its maintenance business as it creates a barrier to 
third-party maintenance providers.   It is a strongly embedded business.  
 
The founders currently own 30% of the company and we believe keep a strong focus on 
shareholder value creation.  SAP currently trades on 15x 2010 earnings versus an 
historic (and DCF justified) multiple closer to 20x.  
 
Oracle 
 
Oracle is a global leader in the database and application software spaces.  Oracle 
dominates the $30bn database market with 50% share, followed by IBM at 21% and 
Microsoft at 17%. In the $30bn Enterprise Applications market, Oracle is second to SAP 
with 20% share.  Oracle derives considerable growth from the faster growth middleware 
segment ($15bn), where Oracle has about 25% of the market with IBM the leader at 
32%.   
 
Following on our diligence from SAP, we concluded that Oracle was a similar business 
with good revenue growth but lower margin expansion potential but on a lower entry 
valuation.  We believe that Oracle’s earnings are very high quality as over 80% of 
operating income is derived from maintenance of its installed base -  65% of this related 
to databases and 35% related to application software.  85% of revenue growth comes 
from cross-selling to the existing customer base (250k strong) where Oracle feels they 
have a substantial opportunity at gaining wallet share.   
 
 
The delay in closing the Sun acquisition has hurt expectations for 2010.  We believe that 
Oracle will generate $1.50 in cash earnings in 2010 ex-Sun, or $1.65 including Sun, at 
current values of 13x (ex-Sun) and 12x including Sun.  We assume no cyclical recovery 
in IT spending on databases, which fell 20% in the last quarter.   
 
Oracle is very efficiently run and is considered the industry benchmark on margins, so 
there is limited operating leverage from current levels.  As such, we forecast high single 
digit earnings growth beyond 2009.

Japan Tobacco 
 
This is close to a 3% position for the fund. 
 
Tobacco sales account for 95% of Japan Tobacco (JT)’s profits as their Pharmaceutical 
and Foods business are almost insignificant from an earnings point of view.  Last year 
International Tobacco, JTI, accounted for half of their total profits but depreciation of 
emerging market currencies reduced the contribution from JTI’s profits to 40% of 
consolidated profits.   
 
Volumes for JTI were slightly down in the first quarter of this fiscal year which was offset 
by strong price increases, up 9% in US$ year on year.  Going forward we believe that 
the growth prospects for JTI are strong as emerging markets - where volumes are 
expected to grow 5% p.a. and net prices are growing close to double digits on the back 
of continued tax hikes - account for 1/3 of profits.  JTI also managed to increase its 
volumes in some developed markets such as Italy and France on the back of market 
share gains. We expect that JTI will contribute close to 60% of Japan Tobacco’s 
consolidated EBITDA in 2 years time.  
 
JT’s stock price suffered dramatically last year as fears over extreme tax hikes on 
cigarettes in Japan destroyed investors’ sentiment.  However, we do believe that gradual 
tax hikes are the most likely scenario.  Hence there is scope for substantial net cigarette 
price increases in Japan because retail cigarette prices are one of the lowest in the 
developed world at roughly $3 per pack.  EBITDA for JT’s domestic tobacco is expected 
to be JPY280bn this year.   
 
The following example shows how tax and manufacturer price rises can be beneficial to 
JT rather than negative as the market assumes.  Assuming cigarette pack retail prices 
are hiked 3x to 900 yen per pack and assume taxes go to 65% of retail prices (from 
58% today) and penetration of smokers to decline by a 40%, then domestic EBITDA will 
more than double to more than JPY600bn, which would mean that the levered free cash 
flow yield goes to 19% for JT.  Even if we assume 30% volume elasticity to massive 
price hikes, penetration of smokers would drop by 60% to 10% of total Japanese 
population, which is probably too conservative.  Nevertheless, the domestic EBITDA 
would still increase to JPY457 from JPY279bn (or 16% levered free cash flow yield) for 
JT.  
 
Japan Tobacco is generating an 11% free cash flow yield on our run rate estimates, 
which is much higher than peers which have levered free cash flow yield closer to 7-8% 
this year. This is especially attractive as long term interest rates in Japan are 2% lower 
than in most other developed economies.  The stock is also cheap versus other 
consumer stocks in Japan, which are trading at an average P/E of 20. 
 
Concerns about poor corporate governance are overly discounted in our opinion. JT will 
gradually raise its dividend payout, which is expected to go to 27% this year (from 23% 
last year).  Profits from their domestic tobacco business have significant upside as retail 
cigarette prices are the lowest in world on PPP adjusted basis.  The government holds a 
majority stake in JT which is likely to be reduced through a share buy back in the

Molson Coors 
 
Molson Coors is a new position we added in the quarter.   It is 3% of fund’s NAV. 
 
The company is one of the world’s larger brewers with major operations in Canada 
(c.40% of profits) and the US (c.50% of profits), with a smaller business in the UK 
(c.10% of profits). It is also one of the cheapest major brewers trading on less than 13x 
earnings against a peer group closer to 15x forward earnings. 
 
The key attraction of this investment is that we feel we are in the first year of a multi-
year profit upgrade and re-rating cycle, led by cost cutting and the potential for real 
pricing principally in the US.  The US has been a difficult market for many years, with 
sub-inflation pricing and two sub-scale players (Miller and Coors), trying to compete with 
themselves and a market share sensitive incumbent (Budweiser).  This has led to beer in 
the US being among the cheapest at retail of the developed nations.  Also, particularly 
within the Miller and Coors systems, a disproportionate share of profits is retained by the 
distribution partners, and Miller and Coors have had substantial scale disadvantages 
versus Budweiser.  
 
Following Inbev’s acquisition of Budweiser and the merger of Miller and Coors 
(MillerCoors is 42% owned by Molson Coors) both in 2008, we feel the industry has for 
the first time created a viable duopoly in the US market, with the two companies holding 
a combined 80% market share.  This, in addition to the relative value of beer in the US 
versus other developed markets indicates at least a favourable pricing outlook over the 
coming years with both companies seeking real price increases in our view. 
 
Moreover, the new MillerCoors business is considerably less efficient than its larger rival 
reflecting the duplicate cost bases and scale disadvantages of its predecessors.   In 
2008, the pro-forma margin of MillerCoors was 10% vs 24% at Inbev North America.  In 
2009, we expect MillerCoors to achieve 15% and for ABInbev to achieve 34%. Over the 
next 2 to 3 years ABInbev targets a margin in the mid to high 30%’s, whereas 
MillerCoors initial target is for a 17% margin.  We believe this gap will close over time, 
and while there should be a margin differential between the two players, there is no 
reason for the number two operator with 30% market share to have less than half the 
margin of the number one with 50% market share.  
 
In terms of valuation, we expect the free cash flow yield to rise to 9-10% over the next 
2-3 years and be approximately 8% in 2010.

Coca-Cola remains one of the strongest and highest return exposures to emerging

Corporate Bonds 
 
Currently the fund has 16% invested in bonds, purchased at prices offering double digit 
returns.  Our largest holdings are bonds of BAA, Lloyds Bank and Barclays Bank.  The 
Barclays Bank bond was bought at 101% of par and pays a 14% fixed coupon, is 10 year 
non-callable and steps up to Libor +14% in 2019.  The Lloyds Bank bond pays a 13% 
coupon stepping up to Libor +14% also and is also 10 year non callable.  Both bonds are 
cumulative in the event of coupon deferral, and do not allow common stock dividends to 
be paid if their coupons are not paid.  In the case of Lloyds, the market fears coupon 
deferral under pressure of the EU to make hybrid capital bear losses.   
 
Our analysis says that, because of the cumulative feature we are likely to make a strong 
double digit return in any realistic scenario. 
In terms of credit quality, we feel that both banks will need to continue to raise capital 
under tremendous pressure from regulators.  Lloyds is expected to launch one of the 
biggest rights issues ever of up to $25bn shortly and participate in the UK government’s 
asset protection insurance scheme for toxic assets.  With a 30% market share of the UK 
banking market and a UK government ownership of 43%, it is too strategic for the UK 
economy to be allowed to fail.   It has been offered the option to put a third of its total 
loans (£260bn out of £700bn total loans) into an insurance scheme where the UK 
government will take 90% of losses after the first 10% loss.  Once it cleans the 
commercial real estate loans of its disastrous HBOS acquisition it has as much as £13bn 
of earnings power pre-provisions from which to pay dividends to shareholders which 
forces interest payments on its bonds.

4. Outlook 
 
The overwhelming consensus view is that the recession has come to an end.  However, 
this does not mean that any recovery in the US and Europe will be robust or sustainable.   
Without job creation or wage growth it is hard for this to be the case.  If most companies 
are going to maintain margins with very aggressive job reductions and consumers 
remain leveraged and in fear of their jobs, where will the top line recovery come from 
except emerging markets? Hourly wages in the US remain stagnant and the U6 
employment measure in the US is now 17%.  The gap between the official 
unemployment rate of 10% and the U6 measure is a record high.  Aggregate hours 
worked in Q3 actually declined 3% in the US.  Eventually, taxes will rise, government 
spending will have to fall and life without stimulus will be tested.  In the UK the 
conservative party has already announced that they cannot afford to pay people 
pensions at 65 and are going to raise the retirement age to 66 if they get into power as 
a small example of what this will mean. 
 
Personal consumption is very unlikely to rise a lot in the US and Europe, given the need 
to deleverage and the temporary nature of the stimulus and rising unemployment.  The 
debt fuelled consumption of the last decade is over.  As for private investment, the 
massive excess capacity giving a record low capacity utilisation of 70% in the US 
suggests we have too much already.   
 
Governments have been providing life support with debt fuelled deficit spending as the 
key growth driver in the US and Europe and China.  However, the price of this is an ever 
weakening dollar which cannot correct the deindustrialisation of America which has 
occurred over the last 20 years.  For now long term interest rates at 3% are supporting 
equity prices but these rates seem inconsistent with the giant budget deficits being run.   
 
Will annual supply of $1-2 trillion of annual net new treasury issuance find enough 
buyers at 3% interest rates when the currency demand from overseas central banks is 
waning as they diversify their reserves into non-dollar currencies?  Without reforming 
entitlements and balancing budgets, eventually the monetisation of government debt will 
become so big that investors will need to care about the real nature of their returns 
(rather than just the nominal) and about their currency choices.   
 
Essentially, the imbalances of the debt fuelled consumption and investment bubble have 
not been corrected and we remain cautious on economies in Europe and the US.  For this 
reason, we have constructed a highly defensive portfolio with utilities, consumer staples 
with exposure to emerging markets and mission critical software and defence companies 
and some high yielding bonds.  We will try to get and remain fully invested if we can find 
enough opportunities since the opportunity cost of being in cash at zero rates is too 
punitive, especially if inflation comes to bear.
