3. Portfolio Review for Q1 2010  
 
 
Visa 
 
Visa‟s share price increased 4% in Q1. 
 
Visa held its first ever investor day in March in San Francisco which three members of 
our investment team attended.  The event was a reasonably comprehensive review of 
Visa‟s perspectives on its own opportunities, challenges and goals.  We felt that the 
company did an excellent job of explaining its strategy to dominate the global payments 
system. 
 
We learnt more about new market opportunities which Visa is aggressively focused on 
exploiting.  Money transfer, pre-paid, international and commercial payments remain 
giant market opportunities.  The competitive and customer dynamics in each segment 
vary, but the cash and business substitution possibilities are very substantial.  The 
segments range from social security and payroll in the pre-paid space, to the substantial 
volumes in the money transfer market, to accounts and working capital management for 
small and medium sized businesses.  
 
Visa‟s current global payment volume is $3trn.  In the context of this they feel the US 
pre-paid cardable opportunity is over $1trn, the commercial payments opportunity is 
$9trn and the cross border money transfer opportunity is $11trn.  It is very difficult to

Visa introduced and demonstrated their RightCliq product for the ecommerce space.  The 
product is an on-line wallet, which allows one click payment for products without having 
to enter all your card details or personal details, similar to paypal.  The simple aspect of 
the product is that when you want to pay on-line with Visa, if you are activated on 
RightCliq, you will be prompted straight to this service for one click payment.  RightCliq 
is something of a defensive move, where it has become increasingly important for the 
company to improve the online consumer payment experience and initiate an online 
payment platform that can evolve more flexibly over time.  The product is a reasonably 
neat fix to the consumer experience gap that had opened up with PayPal.  They expect 
the banks to promote the product actively through online banking channels and direct 
marketing. 
 
Disintermediation remains a constant preoccupation of our studies.  Both mobile and 
online are potential channels for disruptive technologies and competitors.  Visa did not 
allocate much time to mobile, appearing to suggest that while the group is running pilots 
in many parts of the world, the lack of standards and the competing agendas of the 
prospective parties are providing strong headwinds to a mass roll out.  From our 
perspective we think the alignment of Visa with the banking system, the ubiquity of 
acceptance, and reliability of the platform are strong protections for Visa if payments do 
move online, but it remains an area of constant investigation.  
 
The business trends for Visa continue to accelerate out of the downturn as retail sales 
have stabilised and the share of debit in the mix has risen strongly.  US payment volume 
growth troughed in Q2 2009 at negative 3%, and global transactions growth troughed at 
+6%.  These have subsequently accelerated to +11% and +13% respectively in 
December and January.  Cross-border and international trends also continue to improve 
significantly.  
 
Q1 saw revenue growth accelerate to 13%, compared to 10% and 2% in the prior two 
quarters.  We expect this to move up further in the coming quarters as the reported rise 
in payment volume in the US, internationally and cross-border feeds through to the 
profit and loss account.  Global payment volumes, upon which the Q1 revenue is 
calculated, were up only 3%.  This should comfortably rise into the double digits for the 
balance of the year. Similarly current run-rates in cross-border volumes would imply a 
marked acceleration from the Q1 performance, and group revenue should be additionally 
supported by an improvement in the rebates base effect from the prior year (Rebates 
were low in H1 2009, which made the H1 comparisons more difficult in H1 2010.  
Rebates comparisons are more normal for H2, which will further support the reported 
revenue growth). 
 
Pricing has been a strong contributor to Visa‟s revenue growth in the past two quarters.  
The major data processing price adjustment has its‟ anniversary in the fourth quarter, 
but we continue to expect targeted use of this value driver over the coming few years.  
Visa remains a small portion of the total global system revenues (<1% by their 
calculation) and a combination of value share and increasing penetration should drive a 
rise in yields over time.  
 
On our current estimates Visa trades on 21x 2010 and 17x 2011 earnings (on a calendar 
year basis).  We feel the group can achieve 5-10 year compounding of 15-25pc pa based 
on the economic model and extended runway for growth, and it continues to trade at the 
lower end of what we consider to be a comfortable multiple range for the business, and 
clearly below intrinsic multiple bounds.

Lloyds 
 
In February Lloyds Group reported its final results for 2009 which were better than 
expected.  The bottom line was that the company now expects to be profitable in 2010. 
 
In the second half of 2009 impairments declined by 21% HoH, although total 
impairments for the year were significant at £24bn driven by the mortgage book of 
HBOS and corporate loans.  Initial guidance for 2010 loan loss provisions were £15bn 
but in March were indicated to be at least 20% lower for 2010.  This is the result of a low 
interest environment, which is reducing defaults on the retail side.  Ireland remains a 
weak area which account for 4% group loans of which 25% are impaired without loan 
loss provisions.  More significant losses are expected on the corporate side (40% of loan 
book and half of estimated losses) and unsecured loans (5% of loan book but almost 
20% of estimated losses).  Management however feels provisioning has peaked and 
states that it has been strongly declining in the first quarter. 
 
Net interest margins increased to 1.8% in the second half of 2009 from 1.7%.  This was 
due to lower funding costs and better asset pricing as the competitive landscape has 
improved versus pre-crisis levels.  The number of mortgage and credit card providers in 
the UK has come down by more than 90% versus pre-crisis for example.  Guidance is for 
interest margins to improve to 2% in 2010, although we could see some pressure from 
higher funding costs.  If interest rates rise they will be more than offset by higher loan 
yields. 
 
Refunding of the £157bn government support will most likely not be an issue as 
management is planning to reduce the balance sheet by £200bn in the next 5 years 
driven by sales of non-core assets and loan maturation.  
 
Cost trends remain positive and the cost/income ratio improved by 2% in 2009, mostly 
on the back of benefits from synergies with HBOS.  Costs are guided to come down 
further in 2010 and 2011 by another £2bn. This would reduce the cost/income ratio by 
4% to 44%. 
 
Given that Lloyds revised its forecast to a net profit, instead of a loss, it means that loan 
losses are likely to be less than £12bn in 2010.  If this proves correct then Lloyds tier 1 
(under the Contingent Capital Instrument definition) will not go below 9% going forward.  
On top of this we believe that Lloyds is likely to raise equity if their core tier 1 drops 
below 7%, which is the minimum accepted level by the FSA.    Moreover, it is becoming 
increasingly clear that the new Basel 3 regulations that will be introduced over the next 
year will be quite punitive on banks in many ways in requiring them to hold materially

Union Pacific 
 
During the quarter we increased our UP position to 9% of the fund and with the share 
price up +18% YTD it has been the single largest profit contributor in our portfolio this 
year. 
 
UP is the largest railroad in America, and one of the two major railroads in the western 
half of the US (Burlington Northern being the other). UP‟s franchise extends from the 
West coast to around the Mississippi river, and includes more entry points to the US 
(ports and interchanges with Mexico and Canada) than any other US railroad.  
 
In our previous investor letter we mentioned we expected run-rate volumes to deliver 
+7% volume growth in 2010 and that the risk was to the upside.  Throughout the 
quarter, volume trends have continued to improve and YTD volumes have delivered 
+13% growth for Q1.  In addition, the implied FY 2010 run-rate volume growth has 
improved to +11-12%. 
 
We had a meeting with senior UP management in Omaha during the quarter who 
reconfirmed our confidence that this management team will continue to be disciplined on 
pricing and costs.  In addition, the meeting provided us with increased conviction in our 
thesis that sell-side analysts continue to underestimate the operational re-bound 
potential in UP's profitability with the current volume recovery.  Through our assumed 1-
2% long-term volume growth, together with legacy contract re-pricing and +2-3% long-
term core pricing, we expect UP to deliver a mid to high teens total long-term 
shareholder return. We forecast profits per share rising to approximately $7 in 2012 
from $4 in 2009, but this will be dependent on the economy not relapsing in a double dip 
recession, which remains the biggest risk in this investment, however the legacy 
contract re-pricing will help to offset this. 
 
 
Coke 
 
In a surprise move in Q1, Coca-Cola announced an agreed deal to acquire its North 
American bottling operations from CCE. While the recent move by Pepsi to acquire its 
own bottlers had significantly raised the odds of this transaction, the company had 
reiterated its strong commitment to the franchise model.  
 
While it is disappointing that Coca-Cola seemed to be led into this transaction by its 
competitor, the current strategic rationale for vertical integration in North America is 
sound in our view, albeit largely defensive. North America is reasonably unique in the 
global non-alcoholic beverage market for its maturity, complexity, and most importantly, 
competitiveness. The competitiveness in particular has made it increasingly important as 
a competitive differentiator to fully control route to market, where the franchise system 
had entrenched competing self interest and lowered flexibility.  
 
From an equity perspective, we do not feel this changes the outlook dramatically. The 
group‟s US earnings exposure rises from 18% to 24% and the capital intensity of the US 
business rises. On the positive side, the transaction credibly resolves some of the issues 
that have led the US earnings to underperform for so long, emerging market exposure

Oracle 
 
Oracle is a 6% position in the fund and appreciated by 5% in the first quarter. 
 
Most of the Street and the buy-side community remain focused on the recent Sun 
acquisition.  We are positive on the transaction but feel the attention it has garnered is 
disproportionate; Sun cost Oracle $5bn, less than 4% of Oracle‟s market cap.  We have 
had recent conversations with senior management that confirmed an EBIT contribution 
of $1.5bn from Sun in the first full year of ownership.  This leaves us comfortable that 
Oracle made a good acquisition.  Worries over the integration of the deal are weighing 
on the stock and should abate as Oracle executes. 
 
The underlying fundamentals of the applications and database market continue to 
improve as corporate IT spending increases following a tough 2009.  This was evident in 
their recent results where Oracle reported strong top line growth of 7% ex-Sun with 
13% growth in high margin applications and software support.  Operating income 
increased by 13% y-o-y.  Both revenues and earnings were stronger than consensus 
expectations.  Pleasingly we have learnt that several large deals closed shortly after the 
quarter ended, which bodes well for Q4.  The company is clearly taking market share 
away from SAP in the enterprise segment.  Management commentary was positive on 
the outlook and they are particularly excited by the potential of new products; 
specifically Exadata, a data warehousing solution which spans both hardware and 
software.  The visible trend for massive structural increases in data storage and analysis 
capability of business is a powerful long term driver of the database business. 
 
The valuation case is that the stock trades on x 13 our 2011 earnings estimate which 
captures the contribution from Sun including cost cuts.  This would be around an 8pc 
free cash yield that can grow around 5-7pc giving around a 14pc medium term total 
return. 
 
 
Lockheed Martin 
 
Lockheed is a 5.5% position in the fund and appreciated by 10.5% in the first quarter. 
 
President Obama‟s first full defence budget was released in early February and was 
supportive of the defence industry.  R,D,T and E (Research Development, Testing and 
Engineering) and procurement (so called “investment accounts” which is where the bulk 
of contractor funding originates from) increased by 2.6% over 2009.  Forecasts were 
also released to 2020 that show investment accounts growing at 2.5% CAGR from 2010-
2020.  While the very fast increases in defence spending seen under Bush are clearly 
over, declines in the budget look similarly unlikely. 
 
Lockheed Martin hosted an investor day at their aeronautics facility in Texas that allowed 
us access to all management, and to see firsthand how the F-35 was progressing.  The 
F-35 (also called the joint strike fighter (JSF)) dominated the day. The F-35 is by far the 
largest defence procurement ever undertaken at $330bn and it generates commensurate 
media attention.  Revenues to Lockheed Martin are currently modest, but ramp up each 
year and should make up 25% of sales by 2016.

Link Reit 
 
In March it was announced that George Hongchoy will succeed Ian Robins as CEO of Link 
REIT, he is currently CFO. Chief Operating Officer Ross O'Toole also resigned in March 
after two years at Link REIT. Ross will be leaving following a successful search for his 
replacement. A number of business initiatives have been implemented, including moving 
from outsourcing to directly managing the property portfolio. 
 
Despite the changes in management we find Link an attractive investment as valuations 
remain attractive and the company is on track, making steady progress on its Asset 
Enhancement Program to upgrade its shopping malls. Dividend yield is estimated to be 
6% fiscal year ending Mar „11, average rent is expected to grow by 5% CAGR in the next 
5 years and close to double digit growth this year. Top line growth combined with 
greater cost efficiencies should result in even greater profit and dividend yield growth 
projected to be close to 6% CAGR – we estimate the levered IRR to be 13% using a cap 
rate of 4%. 
 
Given Link‟s low gearing, over 6x ebit/interest expenses, 6% dividend yield vs 10 year 
Hong Kong sovereign at 2.9% the risk return is very attractive. Especially considering 
the strength of the local economy; retail sales are very strong this year 8% higher YTD 
vs. the average in 2009 in value terms.   
 
 
Wellpoint 
 
Wellpoint is a 4% position for the fund and appreciated by 10% in Q1. 
 
It has been a turbulent time for the health insurance companies which rose to the top of 
the political agenda as the democrats saw an opportunity to increase support for the bill 
by demonising the industry.  Listening to President Obama‟s speeches, it would be easy 
to conclude that all of the problems of the US healthcare system are due to the 
insurance industry and that the recently passed healthcare reform bill will crush the 
insurers and thus dramatically reduce the costs of coverage.   
 
The reality remains different on both points.  Total healthcare insurance profits are circa 
$13bn, while total healthcare spending is $2.5trn, so health insurance profits are 0.5% 
of total spend. It is possible, but very expensive, for an already indebted US government 
to drive health insurance companies out of business by nationalising this function, but it 
remains an unpopular approach in the US and a public option was excluded from the bill.

The bill has many details which are yet to be finalised, but its overall impact on Wellpoint 
is roughly balanced. 
 
On the positive side: 
 
Roughly 30m extra Americans will have health insurance, and all of that 
insurance will be provided by the health insurance industry. 
 
Tax breaks for small business and hefty fines for large businesses should help 
increase coverage levels. 
 
The government funded high risk pool and minimum mandated benefit levels are 
favourable. 
 
The negatives are: 
 
 
A low individual mandate (the fine for not having insurance), which could de-
stabilise the individual insurance market. 
 
The imposition of minimum MLRs (medical loss ratios- although Wellpoint is 
better placed here than the rest of the industry). 
 
Insurance exchanges (should increase price transparency) 
 
Industry taxes and guaranteed issue (this could hurt profitability in the individual 
market).  
 
The NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners) has been chosen to write 
the detailed rules regarding each of these points and understands the concerns of the 
industry.  The major changes are in the individual market (this accounts for only 6% of 
Wellpoint‟s earnings).  Changes in the corporate market are far more important and 
changes here are slight.  
 
All the news has centred on the healthcare reform bill (or expansion bill as it should be 
called; there is almost no reform but $500bn of tax and $1tr of spend, the balance 
coming from medicare).  However, the underwriting cycle has historically been key to 
profitability and all our checks indicate that pricing is robust across for-profits and not-
for-profits, which should lead to margin expansion. 
 
Wellpoint is trading on under 9x ‟10 PE (adj for excess cash that is announced to be 
returned to shareholders) versus its historic rating of 14x.  We think that we will likely 
see a rerating as fear over healthcare reform subsides, but with no PE expansion we will 
still make at least a 15% IRR if earnings grow at only 5pc since capital requirements for 
growth only reduce total returns by about 1pc.  To put this in context healthcare 
spending last year, despite the crisis, grew at 10pc.   A $3bn share buyback should be 
supportive of the stock in the coming year. 
 
 
4. Outlook 
 
The cyclical recovery in equities continued this quarter.  Peoples‟ expectations on the 
economy have been battered during the crisis creating anxiety and scepticism that 
lacklustre economic performance in the G7 is the best we are going to get.  While we 
continue to be defensive in our choice of investments it is worthwhile pointing out the 
upside scenario is one of improving profits as pent-up demand, inventory re-stocking 
and a recovery in capital spending and continued strong expansion in Asia and emerging 
markets are all feasible.  In addition, we must continue to respect that as long as the 
cheap money stays in place it will be supportive of economic recovery and profit growth. 
 
On the dark side, it remains clear that the massive increase in government debt to avert 
catastrophe and support economies cannot be indefinitely increased.  The deficit 
monetisation likely remains the end game for this debt and deficits.  The situation in 
Greece is a clear reminder that these risks are real and not theoretical. 
 
We are seeing increasing evidence in the companies that we talk to, like Visa and Union 
Pacific, that an economic recovery in the US is gathering some real momentum.  Retail 
sales data just released for March for the US was also very strong.  This is giving us
