Union Pacific 
 
Union Pacific (UP) was up over 17% in the quarter and 28% YTD, making it the largest profit 
contributor YTD, both on an absolute dollar and return on capital employed (ROCE) basis.

perform in-line with our expectations.  UP reported strong Q2 results on July 22nd, beating the 
street’s profit expectations by +14%, mainly driven by higher pricing and higher incremental 
profitability on recovering volumes. 
 
As communicated at our Investor Day conference, the investment thesis is predicated on UP’s 
pricing power, rather than just a volume recovery.  UP reported core pricing of +5% yoy during

Visa 
 
Visa was up 5% during the quarter, continuing to underperform the market.  
 
Visa reported another very solid quarter of operational performance, with double-digit growth in 
nearly all business metrics and contained cost growth, driving modest upgrades to consensus 
estimates.  The business continues to exhibit very strong secular drivers and cyclical resilience.  On 
our current estimates, the business trades at 13.5x forward earnings which, given the diversity of 
the business and growth drivers, comfortably discounts the risks discussed below.  The bottom line 
of our view is that the business is not going to be materially impacted by the regulatory changes 
announced and will continue to be a medium and long-term winner in the electronic payments 
space and grow earnings strongly over the next 5-10 years.   
 
As part of the ongoing due diligence during the summer, one-on-one meetings were held with the 
CEOs of Visa and Mastercard.  At these meetings, it was emphasised that the banks are partners of 
Visa and Mastercard, whereas companies like Paypal are ultimately competitors seeking to take 
interchange away from the banks.  We believe that prices charged to Paypal for use of the banks’ 
ACH (Automated Clearing House) network will significantly increase in the future, making Paypal 
an expensive alternative to companies like Visa, that own their own network.   
 
Another important point highlighted was the potential for Visa to reconfigure its pricing structure, 
to charge more for the highly valuable acceptance network and for payment guarantee functions 
(for which it holds several billion dollars of excess cash) and less for processing fees, which could 
become more competitive.  Additionally, banks are likely, in our opinion, to move more of their 
debit business to charge cards, like Amex, which would allow them to continue charging 
unregulated interchange (which only applies to debit cards, not for longer payment term charge 
cards).

The key variables that remain a subject of market discussion are the regulatory environment, and 
particularly the Fed's interpretation of the Durbin amendment, as well as future regulatory risks, in 
light of the precedents that have been set in the Dodd-Frank Financial Reform Act. 
 
Implementation of the Durbin amendment: The language has left reasonable latitude for 
interpretation by the Fed in two key areas; the cost definitions that constitute the basis for 
interchange calculation and the scope of non-exclusivity.  The Fed is commonly expected to favour 
the financial industry in opting for the least disruptive solution, however there will also be strong 
political and merchant lobbies who will seek the most aggressive interpretation.   
 
Interchange: A common fear is that a draconian cut will lead the banks to turn on Mastercard and 
Visa and seek steep price concessions commensurate with the revenue hit they face on 
interchange.  The international precedents of very aggressive interchange regulation have however 
been broadly benign for the networks.  The networks have been rational on pricing, the banks have 
made up the lost revenue through reward cuts and direct consumer fees, and revenue growth has 
continued unimpaired.  The networks are a very low share of system revenue, a high share of 
system value, and interchange regulation does not increase the banks' bargaining power or options 
when it comes to network choices. 
 
Non-exclusivity: It appears most likely, but not certain, that the Fed will look to interpret the 
relevant clause as meaning ‘a PIN network and a signature network on one card should not be 
affiliated’.  This is a very benign outcome for the regulation, and would mean the ultimate revenue 
exposure would be between 3% and 5%, and the potential revenue hit less than 2%.  
 
On 13.5x earnings the stock has the potential to compound strongly over the medium-term. 
 
If non-exclusivity is expanded to apply to PIN and signature in parallel, then the revenue exposure 
is 20%.  The onus will then be on Visa and Mastercard to unbundle pricing to retain economics on 
components of their contribution to the system that are tough to replicate: breadth of acceptance, 
fraud performance and payment guarantees.  The adjustment will clearly be less smooth than the 
interpretation of non-exclusivity mentioned above, but the question for the merchants will come 
down to whether they can switch Visa off or not, and if not, what their membership fee should be. 
 
A broader non-exclusivity interpretation is possible, but as it applies to 20% of the business, and 
even there where Visa retains strong pricing and cost mitigation levers, we think it is excessively 
discounted in a 13.5x earnings multiple. 
 
Disney 
 
Disney was up 5% during the quarter. 
 
The company reported an uneventful quarter, however investors remain concerned about the Parks 
business, even though this is only about 20% of total Disney profits.  There are two main concerns 
in this segment: firstly increased pension liabilities, which we expect the company to partially 
offset through cost cuts, and secondly slower recovery coming out of the recession, due to the 
weak US consumer. 
 
We remain confident in the Parks business over time.  Historically, in non-recession years, the US 
parks have 20-24% operating margins; even during recessions margins have been in the 15-16% 
band.  We forecast non-recession margins of 20%, and the recession margins (‘10/’11) of 15% and 
17% respectively.  We continue to like these assets, believing they are unimpaired, unique and 
should take pricing over a business cycle. 
 
During the quarter ESPN, which remains the biggest driver of Disney’s growth, signed a new 
distribution contract with Time Warner Cable; we estimate this locks in affiliate fee growth of at 
least 8% per annum over the coming 5 years.  ABC is also starting to charge retransmission fees 
to cable and satellite operators, as part of this ESPN negotiation.

Viacom 
 
Viacom B shares were up 18% in Q3, strongly outperforming YTD both Disney and Time Warner. 
 
Viacom’s programming portfolio showed ratings strength through the quarter.  The turnaround at 
MTV appears underway, demonstrated by ratings significantly exceeding expectations.  This drove 
strength in cable television advertising markets and management have recently commented that 
they expect this strength to continue through CY 2010, with Q3 and Q4 each being sequentially 
stronger than the prior quarter. 
 
Viacom continues to improve monetisation of its content by re-pricing its distribution contracts 
upwards and signing a lucrative long-term deal with Netflix for distribution of its EPIX movie 
network.  We believe that 25% of the company’s contracts will be re-priced upwards over the next 
18 months as they expire and continue to believe the company can increase pricing in the high 
single digits. 
 
We think the company will demonstrate operating leverage, as it eliminates loss-making franchises 
from its portfolio, such as Rock Band and EPIX; should the economy turn negative, we feel 
confident that Viacom will reduce its non-programming costs and focus on margin preservation. 
 
We continue to believe that Viacom is undervalued, given its asset mix, cash flow generation and 
commitment to returning all cash generated to shareholders.  We expect the shares to be 
supported by 1) earnings growth from affiliate fee increases, advertising growth and cost 
rationalization, and 2) Viacom’s recently initiated share buy-back program, which should also 
trigger upgrades to 2010 and 2011 estimates.  Beginning on October 1, we expect $4bn in share 
buy-backs and dividends over the next 24 months, versus a current market cap of $22bn - almost 
25% of outstanding B shares (a smaller per cent of the total market cap due to the A shares 
existence, which are predominantly owned by Sumner Redstone). 
 
Nestlé 
 
Nestlé was flat during Q3 in Swiss Franc terms but, as we did not hedge this position for the Swiss 
Franc, we gained 10.2% in dollar terms net of our Euro hedge. 
 
Nestlé continued to show organic earnings performance and consistency towards the top end of its 
peer group.  This reflects what we feel is a highly robust geographic, market share and category 
mix.  Nestlé trades on 15x forward earnings.  In our opinion, the group has a robust platform to 
consistently deliver high single-digit operating earnings growth at returns on capital of between 
30% and 40%.  We think this delivers very solid long-term absolute shareholder returns off a 15x 
entry multiple, especially accounting for risk vs. the market. 
 
The long-term multiple on this business has been around 17x.  While we do not think this fairly 
reflects intrinsic value given the growth, return, inflation protection and sustainability metrics, we 
do not have a strong view that this will be exceeded and see the returns from the current level in 
the 12-13% band.

Link REIT 
 
Link REIT had a strong quarter and was up 19% driven by a sharp drop in government bond yields 
and strong retail sales in Hong Kong. 
 
At the start of Q3, Link REIT was yielding 5.7% on March 2011 projected dividends vs. the 10-year 
local treasuries at 2.3%; at the end of Q3, it dropped to 4.8% and 2% respectively.  In other 
words, the delta between dividend yield and 10-year Hong Kong treasuries contracted by 60bps, 
but is still very healthy at 2.8%. 
 
During the quarter, the daily correlation between the S&P 500 and Link REIT turned from neutral to 
negative, as the stock benefitted from expectations that interest rates would stay lower for longer 
and a global search for yield.  Retail sales in Hong Kong were very strong, up close to 20% yoy, 
due to a recovery in consumer confidence, as well as increase in Mainland Chinese visitors who are 
taking advantage of lower taxes in Hong Kong.  If retail sales continue to grow at the current pace, 
we see upside risk to our earnings forecasts as well as consensus.  Our recent conversations with 
the CEO support our confidence that they will maintain a positive spread between revenue growth 
of over 5%, and cost growth of more like inflation (2-3%) in the medium-term. 
 
Lloyds  
 
Our position in Lloyds contingent capital bonds was up 17%, including interest payments during 
the quarter. 
 
First half-year results to 30 June 2010 were better than expected.  New mortgages continue to be 
written with much higher net interest margin (NIM) than the back book of mortgages.  As a 
reminder, UK mortgages are around half of the loan book.  Lloyds’ funding situation improved 
modestly, with the loan-to-deposit ratio enhanced by 4% (deposits grew and total loans came off 
marginally).  Core tier 1 improved to 9%, from 8% at the end of 2009. 
 
During September, new Basel 3 proposals for bank capital were announced, making our contingent 
capital bonds safer and also increasing the likelihood that they will be called by Lloyds bank during 
or post 2013.  Basel 3 proposals would lower Lloyds’ pension fund assets, deferred tax assets and 
subsidiary assets reduction to core tier 1 capital and we estimate that pro-forma core tier 1 would 
drop by 1.3% to 7.7%.  It is important to note that the Lloyds contingent capital bonds that we 
own have a 5% core tier 1 trigger, measured using the FSA definitions at the time they were 
issued.  One of the impacts of Basel 3 proposals is to make tighter capital definitions, which 
impacts the amount of capital to be held; both will contribute to make our bonds safer and 
materially reduce the chance of equitisation. 
 
Another critical element of the proposals was the definition of eligible capital for Tier 1 and Tier 2 
(Lloyds contingent capital bonds are Tier 2, because they are dated, i.e. fixed maturity of 2019 and 
have “must pay” coupons, in our case of 15%).  The new definitions on eligibility are not yet 
finalized, but almost certainly are not going to allow triggers for equitisation based on a tier 1 
level.  More likely, this will be based on the regulators’ discretion.  One reason for this, as we

ING Groep NV 
  
ING was added to the portfolio during the quarter, despite the lack of visibility on the economic 
recovery in Europe and the US.  The stock rose 23% during the quarter, although we captured only 
around half of this move. 
 
ING is a global financial institution of Dutch origin, currently offering banking, investments, life 
insurance and retirement services.  During the financial crisis, it received €5bn of capital from the 
Dutch government.  As a result, it was required by the EU to sell off its insurance operations by 
2013 and become a pure European retail and commercial bank.  ING is the #1 retail bank in The 
Netherlands and #1 commercial bank in the Benelux, with a strong direct banking presence in 
Western Europe and North America through its ING Direct subsidiary, a valuable growing asset.   
 
ING is in the process of operationally separating its insurance businesses and prepare it for a spin-
off, which is expected to materialize during 2011. 
 
ING has the potential to double in the next 2 years, based on our sum of the parts.  We expect the 
insurance business to be sold for around its book value (€16bn) and the proceeds used to increase 
capital and repay in full the Dutch government.  We expect the pro-forma core tier 1 ratio to reach 
13%, assuming the sale of the insurance business by the end of 2012.  ING’s current core tier 1 
target is 7.5%, which is above the Basel 3 minimum, and this rises to 9-10% pro-forma end 2010 
for a sale of the insurance business at book.  We do not yet know what premiums, if any, above 
the Basel 3 floor of 7% the Dutch regulators will require, but we expect it will be in the range of 
10-13%. 
 
The bank currently trades at a discount of around 15% to its book value and 5.5 P/E on our 2012 
earnings estimate.  Its US insurance business will have a book value uplift when it restates its 
earnings for US GAAP versus IFRS of up to 20%.  Our assumption is that the insurance business 
will be floated in two separate companies, a US and a non-US.  The latter is a mixture of Benelux, 
Brazil and Korea.  Overall we expect it to be valued at a premium to book value. 
 
Despite the bank’s relatively high exposure to residential mortgages (50% of total loans), we feel 
reasonably comfortable that ING’s provisions are sufficient.  Dutch mortgages account for half their 
mortgage book, which has a very low loss severity (only 2%) with an average 70-80% loan-to-
value and all recourse mortgages; hence, we see limited downside risk.  Exposure to Portuguese, 
Greek, Spanish and Irish government debt is only €3bn out of capital of €30bn.  In the last 10 
years, ING managed to improve its funding mix and increased its cheap deposits from 30% to 45% 
in June 2010.  ING has a clear focus on efficiency and managed to reduce its cost income ratio by 
4% in the last 12 months.  
 
We have met the new management several times.  Jan Hommen, the CEO, was the former CFO of 
Philips, where our analyst Oscar Veldhuijzen used to work for him.  We do not believe that Jan 
Hommen is interested in building an empire and he indicates his expectation in meetings; once the 
insurance business is separated and the capital position normalized, they expect to pay a 
significant dividend.  We estimate they will pay a €1 dividend on EPS in 2013 of over €1.40, giving 
a 12-13% dividend yield on the current share price.

Japan Tobacco 
  
Japan Tobacco (JT) was up 6% in USD terms during the quarter, but flat in Yen terms, despite 
uncertainties related to the introduction of sharply higher retail prices.  
 
As of 1st October 2010, their Japanese cigarette prices were raised by almost 40% at the retail 
level producing a 35% increase in net prices for JT.  We believe that volume sensitivity to higher 
cigarette prices is far lower than guidance and consensus, which should provide a boost to the 
stock when this becomes visible to the market.  
 
JT’s forward P/E is 9x and it is trading on 8x forward EV/EBIT, which is close to 30% discount to 
the sector.  By 2012 we think the stock trades at a 12% close to unlevered free cash yield.  Our 
intrinsic value remains at a significant premium (50-100%) to the current stock price, but the 
strong Yen, low dividend yield and poor Japanese stock market offer little current support to the 
stock. 
 
Oracle 
 
Oracle shares rose 23% during Q3 2010.    
 
During the stock weakness of the summer, we had increased the position to 8% of NAV at prices of 
$21-22.  As a result of the large size of the position and strong performance, it was a top 3 
contributor to the Q3 Fund’s performance.  After the stock rose above $27, we significantly 
reduced the position - even though we still see intrinsic value above $30 - due to a less compelling 
risk/reward. 
 
Shares re-rated from 11x to 13.5x 2011 P/E, after Oracle delivered another set of strong quarterly 
results and Mark Hurd joined the company as co-President.  He had, until recently, been the CEO 
of Hewlett Packard and has an excellent track record as an executive at both NCR and Hewlett 
Packard. 
 
Oracle showed outstanding business execution during the third quarter.  The company reported 
strong results for its fiscal first quarter in September.  Oracle reported clean non-GAAP EPS of 
$0.42, versus expectations of $0.37 driven by strength across all business lines, with new license 
sales growing 25%.  Sales and operating margins beat our expectations, as well as the hardware 
business.  Management indicated there will be structurally higher margins going forward and 
expressed the view that they could double profits from hardware in the medium-term. 
 
We expect the business fundamentals to remain strong, driven by an increase in IT spend globally 
and the launch of several new integrated hardware and software products.  Oracle has shown itself 
to be a disciplined acquirer in the past, however we are concerned by comments regarding 
potential acquisitions in the non-software area and other areas we consider non-core.   
We are also concerned about the company’s growing offshore cash balance, where we see no clear 
goal of repatriation.  Nonetheless, we would hope to have an opportunity to increase the position 
again at lower prices. 
 
Molson Coors 
 
Molson Coors rose 11.5% in Q3 2010. 
 
Molson Coors continued to deliver robustly against its synergy and cost rationalisation goals in the 
second quarter, both in the US and Canada.  As in the prior quarter, the headline benefits of these

the volume environment to stabilise at the very least if not to improve, generating a marked

Early trade data for Q3, and the exit run-rate from Q2, indicate that we are seeing better volumes.   
The unemployment rate appears for the time being stabilized, albeit at a higher level.

Outlook 
 
Whilst the economies have troughed in the West, the recovery has been extremely weak. The 
contrast with the strong growth in Asia ex-Japan and Brazil is marked showing that decoupling of 
emerging markets is real this time around for now. 
 
The situation in the West is summarised by the statement “crisis averted for the moment, but 
problem not solved”.  There remains too much debt and not enough saving in the system, with 
total government and private debt in aggregate running almost at peak levels as aggregate 
demand has been supported by the great swap of increased government debt for reduced private 
sector debt as consumers deleverage. 
 
Nominal income recovery in the US and final sales (taking out the temporary benefit of restocking) 
remains very low.  The contrast between large corporates and individuals in the US is stark.  US 
corporates are generating very large amounts of free cash flow relative to GDP (record amounts) 
and to equity, whilst consumers struggle with their debt levels and fear of unemployment and 
further house price declines.  Is this situation sustainable long term, of companies doing well and 
consumer doing poorly?  One important thing to always bear in mind is that the US consumer 
spending is a small direct proportion of the US stock market earnings and that even in a flat GDP 
world some companies will have secular tailwinds and market share gains.  Our US equity exposure 
remains dominated by stocks with secular growth drivers or cost cutting drivers.
