Lloyds Bank 
 
Lloyds bank bonds performed strongly during the quarter.  One of the drivers was the issuance of 
around $6bn of similar contingent capital bonds by Credit Suisse at an 8% yield, which 
immediately tightened to a 7% yield, after being heavily oversubscribed.  These bonds had a 
relatively high 7% tier 1 trigger under Basel 3, versus the estimated equivalent trigger for Lloyds 
of around 3%.  The lower the trigger, the lower the likelihood of being automatically converted into 
equity, and the greater the chance is that there is a rights issue for equity holders, leaving 
bondholders untouched.  Credit Suisse estimate that their 5% tier 1 trigger contingent capital 
bonds that they expect to issue later this year will come with yields of under 6%.  Currently, our 
bonds trade at over 9% yield to maturity, with optionality from 2012 onwards of the company 
tendering for them and refinancing with much cheaper senior debt. 
 
The new CEO, Antonio Horta-Osorio, formerly CEO of Santander UK, joined the company formally 
in Q1 and is highly regarded.  He is on record saying that he will aggressively shrink the balance 
sheet down to a loan-to-deposit ratio closer to parity, significantly reducing the risk of the bank.  
We also believe that he will likely sell the life insurance division and cut costs aggressively.  We do 
not expect material dividends to be paid for a couple of years, as conservative provisions are taken 
to further de-risk the book in a way that can be blamed on the prior CEO.  We expect all these 
actions to be bondholder friendly, by reducing the risk of the company.   
 
Lloyds‟ market cap is currently $65bn. 
 
QR National 
 
QR National (QR) represents approximately 10% of the Fund‟s NAV.  It is the Fund‟s largest equity 
exposure.   
 
Since its IPO in November 2010, the shares have appreciated 34% in absolute terms.  It is a 
classic privatisation/special situation story which, based on our estimates, should give an 
estimated 15% per annum US dollar 5 year return.   
 
These returns will be driven by re-pricing legacy contracts, significant cost cutting, large quantities 
($6-8bn) of value added growth capex,  leveraging the balance sheet and significant coal volume 
growth in the market place.  By financial year beginning June 2012, we believe the company will 
be on a close to 10% free cash yield ex growth capex and still have significant re-pricing and 
growth to come for many years.  Its significant asset backing of close to $3 per share of tangible 
book value and very conservative balance sheet limits the downside of the investment.  In addition 
half the assets and earnings have very low risk, being a regulated completely asset backed 
business. 
 
At the H1 results the company gave guidance for operating profits for their 2011 financial year of 
around AU$480m on a clean basis (excluding the impact of the floods), which was around AU$80m 
higher than the level forecast at the IPO.  The stock has been performing well, as investors have 
realised that the company has set market expectations unrealistically low and that there are a 
number of self help drivers of profit uplift.  Amongst these drivers are headcount reduction.  During 
Q1 the company announced a voluntary redundancy scheme offering 3,500 workers (35% of the 
total workforce) the opportunity to leave the company in return for 1 year of average 
compensation.  So far 660 workers have accepted the offer.  From 2013 onwards mandatory 
redundancies are allowed under the deal agreed with the Queensland Government and unions prior 
to privatisation.  We estimate that at least 2,000 workers will leave the company in total over the 
next 2-3 years resulting in AU$200m of cost savings. 
 
In addition to headcount reduction, we estimate that procurement savings will reduce costs by 
around a further AU$100m.  The company has hired a new procurement head to address the 
inflated procurement budget of around AU$1.2bn in the coal business.  Prior to privatisation the 
company was required to buy any product that they could in Queensland.

investment.  Roughly half of QR‟s operations comprise the Above Rail business (mainly rolling stock

utilisation and workforce optimisation, which over time should help to create additional shareholder 
value.  During Q1 we met with John Mullen (Asciano‟s new CEO) in London and he emphasised a 
clear goal of pricing new investments in the Above Rail business at 12% after tax, or better, on an 
unlevered basis giving 18% after tax returns to equity on a 50% leverage assumption. 
 
For the Below Rail business (RAB assets), QR has announced progress on a number of rail 
expansion projects which, if successful, should deliver attractive returns above WACC and underpin 
solid earnings growth.  Given the conservative risk/return profile of RAB assets, they can sustain 
significantly leveraged balance sheets.  QR‟s balance sheet was unlevered at the time of the IPO, 
however it will need to considerably re-leverage over time.  This also offers QR the opportunity to 
enhance shareholder value through judicious share buy-backs over time.   
 
We are happy to attach a copy of a presentation that we have made to the company‟s board, 
showing our views on leverage capacity of the company and the significant value added that can 
be achieved from leveraging a balance sheet that when the company privatised in November 2010 
was almost debt free.  
 
US Media (Disney, CBS Corp, Viacom) 
  
The Fund has three investments in Media, which we have carried over from 2010: Disney, Viacom 
and CBS.   
 
The stocks and the sector have performed very strongly in the first quarter, substantially 
outperforming the market, with Disney and Viacom up 16% and 17% respectively and CBS up 
31%.  The major change to our investments in the sector has been to increase our position in CBS 
from 4% to 9%, otherwise our exposures have not changed dramatically from prior quarters.  
 
Our research diligence during the quarter included multiple meetings with senior management and 
owners of the major domestic and international media conglomerates, as well as ongoing 
discussions with media buyers.  We also participated in both the Disney and CBS Investor Day 
conferences.

Disney, CBS and Viacom differ in their relative exposures to the core revenue components, being 
affiliate, retransmission, syndication and advertising.  However, the primary drivers of performance 
in each case are common: the rising scarcity value of premium content, coupled with a sharp 
cyclical recovery in advertising and accelerating competition among existing and new distributors.  
 
Advertising prices have rebounded sharply over the past three quarters and scatter prices are 
currently running 30-40% ahead of the upfront.  Not only does this drive significant revenue 
opportunity in the running quarters, but puts the group in a very strong position to negotiate 
upfront prices for next year.  Importantly, from a macro perspective, this recovery has not yet 
brought advertising back to trend versus GDP following the collapse in 2009, so we feel this growth 
has a solid basis.  
 
CBS is proportionately the most exposed to advertising, which drives the fastest upgrade cycle for 
the group.  Nevertheless, we think that Broadcasters (and CBS as currently preeminent among 
them) have the greatest incremental monetisation opportunities from distributors.  The biggest 
near and mid-term sources of this will be retransmission fees and revenues from alternative 
distributors, which alone could bring up to $800m each of extra revenue and profits to CBS over 
the 5 years.  Additional retransmission fees of $300m will be seen in 2011 and 2012. 
 
Disney‟s ESPN asset continues to grow strongly.  Live sports is the strongest major category for 
media buyers, driving best-in-class advertising growth, and ESPNs ratings continue to advance 
particularly in the most important demos.  Meanwhile, affiliate fee pricing continues to be high 
single digit.  Disney‟s leverage to advertising is more moderate than its peers, because of the 
relative size of the affiliate fee revenue base, and the large consumer product and parks 
businesses.  However, growth rates well into the double digits and near 100% contribution margins 
in advertising are driving very strong earnings growth.  
 
The Disney media and consumer products franchises were a core focus of the Investor Day.  
Disney has a uniquely strong position in a highly consolidated market for kids‟ media, and can 
drive high value and persistency in core franchises well ahead of peers, by monetising them across 
networks, film, consumer products and parks. 
 
A further plank of our Disney thesis is that Park margins will recover faster than consensus 
currently models and approach, if not exceed, prior peaks; a level about which consensus appears 
sceptical.  We think this is driven by headline pricing, reduced discounts and cost cuts.  There is 
clear evidence that pricing and occupancy are all improving in the park business.  The US parks 
business also has multiple precedents of a margin cycle going into and out of recession, which we 
believe is not significantly different this time around. 
 
Network performance at Viacom in primetime has been exceptionally strong.  MTV, Comedy 
Channel and BET in particular, continue to post very strong primetime ratings of +10% YTD with 
MTV up in some months +80% year-on-year.  The CEO described his position going into the 
upfront negotiations as potentially the strongest in memory.  On around 10x 2012 free cash flow, 
and a solid growth base of affiliate fees, this performance should continue to drive very attractive 
earnings growth in the near term, which we believe should be re-rated.  
 
CBS and Viacom have embedded what we perceive to be a number of call options on corporate 
activity; both are aggressively buying back shares.  At least one is probable to be a midterm 
consolidation target in an industry actively seeking consolidation and increasingly lacking large 
scale targets.  Finally, CBS may be looking to sell its Outdoor Advertising (billboard) business and 
the proceeds would likely be reinvested in its own shares.  The CBS Outdoor business is a strong 
and a rare asset, which we value at a significant premium to current trading multiple of the group, 
so we believe that if this transaction happens, it would unlock a lot of value. 
 
We see one year returns of 25% in Disney, 30% in Viacom and 40% to 50% in CBS.  The returns 
in the latter two exclude the optionality due to corporate activity mentioned above. 
 
Our estimates are some way ahead of consensus for the group, reflecting confidence in 
retransmission economics, slightly higher advertising estimates, a park beat for Disney and the 
buyback at Viacom.

SAP  
 
SAP performed very well in Q1 2011 (up 14%) and we added to our year-end position ahead of 
very strong Q4 numbers, making it now an 8% position for the Fund.   
 
SAP‟s large software orders are late cycle and from the checks that we made, we expected 
evidence of the recovery to come through in the Q4 report.  This did indeed occur, with total 
software sales increasing by 20% in constant currencies and adjusted Q4 EPS up 45% year-on-
year. 
 
Management provided strongly positive commentary on the business environment.  The CEO said 
“We showed rock-solid revenue growth across the globe, particularly in the fast growing emerging 
markets... We have excellent momentum and we are confident in 2011 and beyond”.  They 
reiterated their medium-term margin target of 35% (32% in 2010) and forecast organic software 
growth of at least 10% this year.  Following a one-on-one meeting with the CEO at Davos in 
January, my confidence in the business trends increased and I consider them to be extremely 
robust. 
 
Strong business conditions for the industry have continued into 2011.  Oracle reported their fiscal 
Q3 on March 24th (this covers the period Nov-Feb) and had blow-out software applications sales, 
up 34% year-on-year (25% organic growth).  Accenture‟s numbers were also strong.  We have 
reinitiated a modest position in Oracle but prefer the greater complexity and customisation of the 
applications business, where there is an effective duopoly with SAP, compared to the database 
business of Oracle. 
 
While predicting individual quarters for SAP is tricky (large licence sales in the last few days can 
alter the result) we are clearly seeing acceleration in IT spending, which bodes well for the 
remainder of the year.  SAP has two new products (HANA and Business-by-Design) which may 
improve their growth above management guidance for the next two years.  HANA (High 
Performance Analytical Appliance software, which allows very rapid analysis of huge data feeds) is 
testing very well with strategic clients.  Business-by-Design is their cloud-based ERP offering for 
mid-sized companies.  This has been an area of struggle for SAP in the past, but management are 
quietly confident of building a substantial recurring mid-market business with their new offering. 
 
Although the stock has moved up, the discounted cash flow value, based on 3% long term license 
growth and a 10% discount rate, is still 50% higher than the current price, so we still see the 
company as an excellent long term compounder with massive barriers to entry and growth without 
capital. 
 
Coal India 
 
Coal India Limited (CIL) was up 10% this quarter.   
 
The key event of the quarter occurred in February, when the company raised prices by an average 
of 13%.  Prices for non-power consumers were raised by 30-100%, while prices for A-B coal 
grades were hiked by 100% for all consumers.  This is the first time that CIL has raised its prices 
without it being driven by a specific cost increase and adds support to our view that over time the 
majority of the coal that it currently sells at under half world market prices will re-price to 
eliminate this huge discount.  Until this event the almost universal market view was that the Indian 
Government, which controls 90% of the company, would not allow any re-pricing. 
 
CIL raised prices on only 40% of its total production, focusing on the non-power market 
customers.  We believe that there will be another price hike later this year to pass on the 5 yearly 
expected wage increase and, possibly, more.

Market prices for thermal coal reached record levels and Japanese power producers recently settled 
on annual contracts at a record US$130/t fob for 6,000 kcal/kg.  
 
Pressure is building on the Ministry of Environment to ease its stance on environmental clearances 
for additional mining sites.  This is the result of mounting problems amongst CIL‟s customers 
facing coal shortages, as demand growth is already outstripping supply growth. Recently some 
progress has been made on this matter and CIL raised its off take target to 454m tonnes for the 
year ending Mar ‟12, which is an increase of 4-5% year-on-year.  Medium term production and 
sales growth is likely to accelerate, as there are limited alternatives for Indian coal consumers 
other than to source their coal from CIL. 
 
One of the interesting upsides not understood by the market is the steady closure of economically 
unviable underground mines, which are currently heavily loss-making, versus the open cast mines, 
which are making over $5bn of operating profits, we estimate.  The majority of the work force are 
currently employed in the underground mines despite these mines representing only 10% of 
production volume. 
 
Valuations are still very attractive as the unlevered free cash flow yield is 8% in 2011, going to 
10% in 2012 and is expected to double in 4 years time.  The company has an estimated $9bn of 
net cash, no debt and will increase net income from $3bn to $12bn over the next 6 years.   
 
On 12.5x our estimated 2012 earnings plus cash we see 35% upside on a one year view. 
 
CESP 
 
CESP performed strongly in Q1, up 15% in BRL terms and 18% in USD terms.  
 
The main driver of this was positive news flow on the concession renewal and the potential 
privatisation of the company.  
 
CESP‟s FY 2010 results revealed strong underlying free cash flow generation of R1.2bn (12% 
yield), further de-gearing of R1.0bn (net debt/EBITDA is now 2.0x which is in line with the 
company target) and the potential for rapid acceleration of dividends in 2011 and beyond.  
 
We expect to see continuing positive news flow on the industries concession renewal in H1 2011.  
The Government has stated that it wishes to renew the concessions as a point of principle.  It was 
recently reported that the companies are about to meet with the Ministry of Energy to advance 
discussions towards a negotiated settlement.  The Federal Government aims to conclude the 
renewal process in 2011.  The FY results also revealed that the move to IFRS accounting has added 
cR3bn to NAV (net increase of R9/share to R39 per share), which should be positive for the 
concession renewal terms. 
 
The São Paulo Government has recently reiterated its intention to privatise CESP, once the 
concessions have been renewed.  CESP has recently appointed a new CEO, who had been a former 
CEO and energy minister in São Paulo state.  His recent statements suggest management will 
focus on efficiency improvement and the maximisation of cash flow and dividends. 
 
Despite the strong share price performance in Q1, we still see a very attractive long-term real IRR 
of 16% and 40-50% upside to fair privatisation value.

Enagas maintained its dividend payout at 60% of earnings but we now expect this to increase to at 
least 65%, given Red Electrica‟s move to this level post the Enagas results.

Red Electrica 
 
Red Electrica performed well, up 16% including dividends during the quarter.  This is due to strong 
year end results and outlook, and improving sentiment towards Spanish sovereign risk.  Spanish 
10 year bond yields (around 5%) have diverged from those of Portugal (now touching an 
unsustainable 10%). 
 
FY 2010 results included a 19% increase to EBITDA, 30% to EBIT and 16% to EPS in underlying 
terms to 3.14/share.  We expect the outlook for 2011 to remain robust, with EPS growth of 13% 
and a medium term CAGR in line with this level. 
 
In its strategy update, management reiterated its capex plan of €800m pa to 2015 but positively 
surprised by extending this to 2020 (expected to be formalised in the National Energy Plan in 
November 2011).  High capex is good for earnings growth and protection against regulatory 
change.  In addition, Standard Values of capex and opex (the basis on which Red Electrica is 
remunerated) were finally approved and in-line with current actual, so a downside regulatory risk 
has now been removed (Red Electrica has been waiting nearly 3 years for this). 
 
Red Electrica also positively surprised on dividends, increasing its payout ratio to 65% (from 60%) 
and maintaining this higher payout going forward.  
 
Red Electrica trades on calendar 2011 PER of 11.3x and a DPS yield of around 6%.  On current 
growth expectations, 2013 P/E falls to 9.0x and DPS yield of 7.2%.   
 
We still see around 30% upside to fair value. 
 
Consumer Staples (Danone, Unilever, Coca Cola) 
 
Danone, Coca-Cola and Unilever share prices have been broadly flat for the quarter adjusting for 
currencies (+/- 2%), although we added positions in Unilever and Danone mid-quarter at weaker 
levels.  
 
The Fund‟s exposures to the sector have changed materially since the back half of 2010, selling 
down Nestlé, while building on positions in Danone and Unilever through the current quarter as 
commodity cost fears accelerated. 
 
Coming into the year the consumer staples sector has suffered from sharp rises in soft commodity 
prices, a function of a strained agricultural supply chain, exacerbated by droughts in Russia, 
Australia and China.  As the benchmark commodity baskets rose by around 10-15% for household 
and personal care businesses and by 30-40% for many food companies, investors worried that 
margins would be squeezed.  
 
We believe that the profitability of companies with the right category and geographic exposure will 
prove resilient, much as they did do in 2008, when the environment was somewhat worse, and the 
preparedness of consumers to absorb swings in costs was lower.  We think that commodity cost 
fears have actually presented an opportunity to build positions in a select number of very strong, 
high return, long duration franchises and the risks are excessively discounted.  If the business 
models do again show resilience to a pretty extreme commodity environment, it should reassure 
the market of the quality of the franchises and permit a re-rating. 
 
We think Danone and Unilever have multiple levers to address the spike in commodity costs from

Wellpoint 
 
Wellpoint was up 23% in Q1, catching up on a very weak performance in 2010. 
 
The big driver of the share price performance was a realisation by investors that healthcare reform 
does not and will not mean the end of managed care companies and that there are opportunities

We had a frustrating year owning the stock in 2010 - our analysis of the Bill in late 2009 showed

Q4 results for Wellpoint were strong, with EPS up 15% year-on-year despite the increase in

increase their market share slightly, while pricing ahead of medical cost inflation.  Market share

The introduction of minimum medical loss ratios has limited the margins that the industry can 
make, but it has also made it far less attractive for a company to price to win market share, as 
companies will not be able to recoup these costs in a low medical inflation year.  This has caused 
industry pricing to be very conservative and has blunted competition between plans. 
 
Valuation of the stock is still low.  The company trades on a 2011 free cash flow yield of over 10%.

United Parcel Service 
 
We initiated a position in United Parcel Service (UPS) during the quarter. 
 
UPS is the largest package delivery company in the world.  It operates in 3 divisions: (i) US 
domestic package, which is the delivery of parcels domestically by truck or air, (ii) International 
package, delivering to more than 200 countries worldwide and (iii) Supply Chain & Freight, which 
includes freight forwarding, contract logistics, and a less-than-truckload business.  Approximately 
6% of US GDP moves through UPS‟s integrated parcel network. 
 
The structure of the US package delivery market changed dramatically in 2009, when DHL finally 
exited the market.  DHL had taken over Airborne Express in 2003, in an attempt to break into the 
US market and, after 5 years, had achieved an 8% market share and cumulatively lost about 
$10bn.  DHL could not match the performance, scale or service of UPS and FedEx and so had 
marketed itself as the low-priced competitor.  This created a long period of aggressive price 
competition within the industry and some irrational behaviour (DHL‟s next-day air prices had 
sometimes been priced below UPS‟s 5-day ground product).  In this environment, UPS struggled 
and when volumes declined during the recession its domestic margins fell from 16% in 2006 to 8% 
in 2009. 
 
After an initial land-grab for DHL‟s business in 2009, the market has now evolved into a newly 
rational duopoly, with UPS and FedEx controlling nearly 90% of the market (the US Postal Service 
makes up the balance).  Both companies have committed to „a multi-year yield management 
strategy‟, a euphemism for raising prices. 
 
Pre-DHL‟s exit, UPS had captured around 70% of its announced list-price increases (as large 
customers tend to get discounted rates).  The price rise in January 2011 was 5%, so any return to 
previous capture rates should result in real pricing for the company for the first time in many years 
(its cost base inflates at under 2% due to fixed union contracts and productivity initiatives).

New Proposed Fund Terms 
 
To reward your loyalty and continued support over the years, we are delighted to communicate our 
intention to introduce a hurdle rate on the incentive fees charged by the Fund, such that incentive 
fees would only be charged on any performance in excess of a hurdle.

rate.   
 
It is likely that the hurdle concept will be implemented by introducing four new hurdle classes 
which are equivalent to the existing classes (A, B, C and D), except for the inclusion of a hurdle 
rate in the calculation of the incentive fee.  Investors will be able to exchange their existing 
holdings into the new classes once they reach their existing high watermarks.  The existing 
commitment periods in relation to classes A, B and C will not be reset. 
 
We are also looking to smooth the overall liquidity profile of the Fund‟s capital for the benefit of all 
investors.   
 
We would like to migrate the class D calendar quarter dealing days to quarterly dealing days 
determined by the acquisition date of your holding, i.e. if your holding was acquired on 1 February, 
the new quarterly dealing days would be 1 February, 1 May, 1 August and 1 November.  
 
There will be no change to the redemption dealing frequency or the 3 months‟ notice of redemption 
for class D.   
 
We would welcome any feedback you may have on the proposal.  We hope to be in a position to 
implement the changes during this summer.
