Portfolio Review for Q2 2018 
 
 
21st Century Fox/Walt Disney 
 
21st Century Fox is currently a 31% position for the fund. Funds managed by TCI Fund Management 
Ltd currently hold a 7.4% stake of the equity of the company.  
 
The share price performed very strongly in Q2, rising 35% as Comcast and Disney entered a bidding 
war for the assets currently being auctioned by Fox. This bidding war opened up in earnest after the 
unequivocally positive conclusion of the AT&T–Time Warner trial, where the government lost its suit 
to block the vertical merger of a content group (Time Warner) with a payTV and consumer internet 
operator (AT&T).  
 
Below, we lay out the timeline that has led to the auction, and the role that TCI has aimed to play. 
TCI wrote two public letters to Rupert Murdoch during the quarter. We feel that as the largest 
shareholder in Fox after the Murdoch family, we are well placed to publicly hold the Fox board to its 
fiduciary responsibilities to maximise shareholder value in this process and to set aside potential 
conflicts of interest.  
 
On May 23, Comcast issued a press release stating that it was in the advanced stages of preparing 
an all-cash bid for the Fox assets. On that same day, we wrote a public letter addressed to Rupert 
Murdoch in which we said that we would hold the board of Fox accountable for managing a full and 
fair auction for the Fox assets, in light of Comcast’s stated interest to participate.  
 
We felt our initial letter was important for four reasons: 
 
1. The Murdoch family and the Fox board are contractually restricted by Disney in their 
abilities to either solicit or encourage alternative bids 
2. The board is contractually required to make best efforts to move the Disney bid forward to 
the extent that it does not conflict with their fiduciary responsibilities 
3. The Murdoch family themselves have potential conflicts of interests in weighing the highest 
price against the potential tax efficiency of a Disney offer 
4. We felt the fears around antitrust risks to a Comcast bid, even in advance of the AT&T–Time 
Warner ruling were exaggerated and a more reasoned discussion around antitrust was 
required 
 
In this context we felt we should state our intention to hold the board accountable for its fiduciary 
responsibilities to all shareholders, not just family ones.  
 
On June 12, Judge Richard Leon issued a strong ruling, in which he stated the Department of Justice 
had failed to make the case that the combination of AT&T and Time Warner was anti-competitive 
and would lead to consumer harm. Thus he would allow the vertical merger of a content and network 
group with a payTV and internet service provider to proceed without any conditions. This case has 
set a strong positive precedent for vertical mergers and has gone a long way towards narrowing the 
antitrust advantage that Disney may have had over Comcast in the pursuit of Fox. 
 
On June 13, the day following the AT&T–Time Warner ruling, Comcast announced that it would bid 
$35 per Fox share in cash for the assets being sold by 21st Century Fox, a 19% premium to the 
prevailing Disney equity bid of $28. Comcast also announced that it would at least match the 
regulatory commitments made by Disney and that it perceived that the antitrust risk of the two 
alternative bids was equal and low.  
 
On June 20, Disney raised its offer from $28 in stock, to $38 per Fox share. The raised offer would 
be 50% cash, 50% stock, and there would be a collar on the stock component to partially protect 
Fox shareholders from declines in the Disney share price.

On June 28, the Department of Justice approved Disney’s deal to acquire the majority of the Fox 
assets contingent on the sale of Fox’s regional sports network portfolio. This has been a clear victory 
for Disney, who has achieved US regulatory clearance for a reasonably complex deal in record time. 
The approval followed the June 27 publication of the revised Disney–Fox proxy which laid out the 
terms of the revised $38 bid and reasons that they continued to feel a Comcast bid would face a 
very high antitrust hurdle, notwithstanding the conclusion of the AT&T–Time Warner trial.  
 
On June 28, we published a second open letter to Rupert Murdoch which stated that we strongly 
disagreed with their arguments for heavily discounting a Comcast bid on antitrust grounds and that 
the Fox Board should reconsider which theories of harm against Comcast were truly viable.  
 
We continue to feel the auction should focus on price, given manageable antitrust risks. 
Notwithstanding the early agreement between the DoJ and Disney to approve the latter’s bid for the 
Fox assets, we continue to believe the antitrust risk of a Comcast bid is low and manageable. 
 
There are four relevant axes of consideration when it comes to US antitrust: 
 
1. Horizontal combination of cable network and content companies (in this instance Fox with 
either NBCU or Disney) 
2. Vertical combination of a payTV provider with a content company 
3. Vertical combination of an internet service provider with a content company  
4. Structural remedies: The appetite of the acquirer to address any friction points with 
disposals 
 
We think Comcast can comfortably address all of these. Post the precedent of the AT&T trial and the 
June 27 Disney settlement with the DoJ, and in light of the FCC’s position on net neutrality and 
Comcast’s openness to dispose of the regional sports networks, we do not believe there are theories 
of harm against Comcast that are truly viable. 
 
1. Horizontal antitrust issues for Comcast are no greater than for Disney, and in most 
instances are lower. In the DoJ complaint against AT&T, Turner Networks are ranked second 
only to Disney’s, and thus presumably both cable network families are more important than 
those of NBCU. In which instance, the hurdle for combining with NBCU should be lower.  
2. The announced settlement on June 27 between Disney and the DoJ sets a strong precedent 
and template for a Comcast deal, given the striking similarities of the NBCU and Disney 
operations.  
3. Post the sale of the regional sports networks, and potentially Hulu, Fox in the US is left only 
with the TV and movie studios, as well as the FX and National Geographic cable networks. It 
is difficult to argue that any of these have market power and in fact they clearly operate in 
highly competitive markets.  
4. The outcome of the AT&T–Time Warner trial we believe is unequivocally positive for issues 
of vertical integration of content with a payTV platform. Mostly importantly, unlike DirecTV, 
Comcast is NOT a national distributor, a key premise in the government’s case for harm. 
More importantly, excluding the RSNs, Fox does not have ‘must carry’ content, a necessary 
plank in any antitrust case.  
5. Any argument on net neutrality, and potential anti-competitive harms from combining 
content with an internet service provider, is in stark conflict with the position that the 
government’s own relevant expert agency, the FCC, has taken on the issue. We would also 
note the absence of net neutrality arguments in the AT&T trial, and the absence of 
meaningful historical instances of bad behaviour among ISPs.  
6. 21 Century Fox is substantially an international (i.e. non-US) business, so a major part of 
the value and the strategic rationale for the deal lies outside the jurisdiction of the DoJ.  
Comcast’s hand will be considerably strengthened if they can get the DoJ to move towards an 
approval similar to that with Disney in the near term, but we feel that price should be the dominant 
factor given the largely international nature of the asset being acquired and the antitrust 
considerations laid out above.

Time to close should not be a differentiator 
Even with an accelerated US approval, a Disney transaction will be constrained by the timetable of 
international approvals, which we would expect to take between six and twelve months. Comcast 
should not be on a significantly dissimilar timetable internationally. On the merits, neither Comcast 
nor Disney have significant hurdles to international approval, but the process will still take time.  
 
Transaction remains highly strategic and mutually exclusive for both acquirers  
Below we summarise the strategic matrix for Disney, but one could easily replace Disney with NBCU 
(Comcast) in the first column and achieve the same picture.  
 
Ultimately this transaction is about creating a credible Netflix competitor urgently. For that, the 
acquirers need Direct to Consumer scale, which only Fox can bring with its stakes in Hulu, Star and 
Sky, and content depth which is achieved through the combination of the Fox studio with that of the 
acquirer.  
 
 
 
We expect Comcast and Disney to compete hard because there can only be one winner of this asset. 
The strategic assets are very difficult to split and collusion between the buyers is prohibited 
contractually and by antitrust law.  
 
New Fox investment thesis remains unchanged 
New Fox is currently trading at an implied price of $11 per Fox share based on the current Disney 
bid, and we believe it is worth $16, based on strong monetisation upside over the coming 3-5 years 
and a 12x exit multiple.  
 
1. 
New Fox is significantly undervalued, trading on less than 8x FY20 FCF 
2. 
Fox News is around three-quarters of New Fox EBITDA. We believe Fox News is among the 
strongest assets in legacy media, with a consistent and passionate viewership, still under-
monetised, and an extremely attractive economic model with mid-sixties EBITDA margins 
underpinned by very strong brand equity 
3. 
The Fox Broadcast Network is recasting itself as a sports network, with now the strongest 
portfolio of sports rights among any of the major network groups 
4. 
We believe Fox News, Fox Broadcast and Fox Sports are ‘must carry’ in any slimmed-down 
bundle and in aggregate the New Fox family of networks represents the most compelling price 
value equation for distributors in the current bundle. This drives our expectation of 
retransmission and affiliate upside 
 
Returns: We expect upside of between 10% and 35% dependent on Comcast’s return 
Based on the current $38 Disney offer for RemainCo and our fair value for New Fox of $16 per share, 
we see 10% upside to $54 per Fox share on the gross long position. While this upside is modest, we

Charter 
 
At the time of writing Charter is broadly flat with the share price level at the start of the second 
quarter. However, the second quarter was an extremely volatile one, with a sharp decline post 
somewhat weak first quarter results, and a subsequent recovery. Following reductions in our Charter 
position over the preceding few quarters, we took advantage of the share price weakness in Q2, and 
increased the position meaningfully. Charter is currently an 18% position for the fund.  
 
Q1 results 
The Charter share price had a negative reaction to the company’s Q1 results declining 12% on the 
day. We believe the reaction was excessive.  
 
At a high level financial performance was solid with modest acceleration in revenue and EBITDA 
growth. However, the key area of sensitivity was the deceleration in broadband subscriber and 
customer relationship growth versus the prior year. Importantly, this deceleration happened in a 
period when the management team had guided the market to expect acceleration. The extremely 
poor communication, combined with broader concerns about competition, and the erosion of the last 
vestiges of takeover speculation contributed to the very sharp share price decline on the day.  
 
Below we summarise the Q1 results in the context of the preceding quarter’s performance and our 
forecasts for the coming years. 
 
 
 
Key points 
1. At a high level this deceleration is small, -60bps in growth from Q4’s 5.5%. This is not a 
collapse in sub trends, and not miles away from where we are for the year, but investors 
are highly sensitive especially given such explicit guidance that they are now being asked to 
wait another quarter or two to see acceleration 
2. Management blamed an integration error last year which saw a billing system accept low 
credit score subs on a post-paid rather than pre-paid basis. When corrected in Q1, they saw 
these subs churn off and not pay their bills. They expect this effect to taper in Q2, and 
disappear in Q3, and say the underlying trends are as expected 
3. If it is 100k subs, that would have contributed an extra 50-60bps to broadband and 
customer growth, which would have put it more in line with the end of last year 
4. At the same time they are indicating growth in gross adds, which would imply as the churn 
abates that we get solid acceleration in subscribers 
5. Charter still has a relatively low market share in its footprint (45% of homes passed) given 
their infrastructure advantages, and we think they have scope to move that number to 55% 
or higher over the medium term

6. Video is progressively less relevant, and management increasingly refer to that. Higher sub

7. Capex was high, but will decline meaningfully in 2019 and again in 2020 as set top boxes

Mobile launch 
At the end of the second quarter, Charter formerly launched their mobile product. Spectrum Mobile 
will cost $45 per line for unlimited and $14 per GB for limited plans. This pricing is similar to 
Comcast’s, and going into 2019, we expect the cable industry to push aggressively in wireless.  
 
We think that cable will become a meaningful player in the mobile market over the next 5 years, 
driven by a compelling value proposition to consumers in terms of lower prices, and network 
convergence at the infrastructure level where we believe they have significant advantages in 
delivering 5G in most of the country given the unique existing dense infrastructure.  
 
We have detailed our thoughts on the mobile entry and its characteristics in previous letters, but 
ultimately we believe this free cash flow could be worth $20–35bn in 5–10 years, versus the current 
market cap of $90bn. 
 
Returns 
We continue to feel that Charter has considerable value drivers at its disposal over the next 5 years 
as management execute their plan to integrate Time Warner Cable, Charter and BrightHouse. We 
believe that as Spectrum pricing and packaging is rolled out across the base, subscriber growth will 
accelerate and capital and opex intensity will decline, whilst the operating drivers are being 
complemented by aggressive share buybacks. In the medium term, Charter’s relatively low 
broadband price points (Charter prices at a $15–25 discount vs Comcast in their respective 
footprints) and low starting video penetration are sources of strategic and value upside.  
 
In terms of broadband penetration, Charter is largely unchallenged in 75% of its footprint, with a 
superior product and often a cheaper price. Long term we think this infrastructure advantage should 
allow them to drive penetration to at least 55% from the 45% today. This should allow consistent 
broadband subscriber growth of more than 5% and customer growth of around 3%. Importantly, we 
believe our penetration and pricing assumptions are consistent, with a gradual rise in the portion of 
the country which can offer competitive fixed infrastructure, whether this be fibre to the home or 5G 
fixed wireless to around 40% of the footprint versus 20% today.  
 
We forecast $40 free cash flow per share in 2021. Based on a 15x exit multiple we think the shares 
can get to $600 in 3 years, an annualised rate of return of 27%.  
 
 
Altaba 
 
The fund’s P&L on the Altaba/Alibaba position was flat in Q2 2018. 
 
TCI’s activism was the key driver of Altaba’s announcement of a $15bn tender offer in June. We 
estimate that the tender offer is approximately 2% accretive to the after-tax NAV of Altaba. The 
tender offer is expected to be completed by early August. The initial premium offered was 4% which 
drove strong interest from arbitrageurs.  
 
We expect Altaba to announce subsequent steps to unlock shareholder value after the current tender 
offer is completed. Altaba could pursue a series of exchange offers which would culminate in the 
liquidation of Altaba. Alternatively, Altaba could switch to a full liquidation with blockage discount.

Firstly, rail transportation is four times more fuel efficient than trucking and shippers attempting to 
lower their carbon footprint are allocating more of their volumes to the railroads. Only about 10% of 
US freight is currently shipped by rail so there is still a huge amount of market share to be gained.  
 
US trucking companies are also facing higher costs of doing business due to new regulations. 
Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs) are now compulsory for all trucks and this will limit the number of 
hours that drivers can work (11 hours a day). Stricter drug testing of drivers has also recently been 
introduced. As a result there is now a significant shortage of truck drivers in the US which is pushing 
up driver pay. Consequently, trucking companies have increased their rates by around 20% yoy 
which has created a favourable environment for rail pricing (rail is typically priced 10-20% cheaper 
than truck transportation).  
 
Rail volumes are up about 4% YTD and margins should expand across the industry due to operating 
leverage and strong pricing (+3-4%).  
 
We met with the CEO of Canadian Pacific during the quarter and also the CFOs of Union Pacific and 
Norfolk Southern. They highlighted the benign pricing environment and the opportunities to increase 
margins.

Norfolk Southern has the lowest margin (33%) in the industry, over 300bps worse than its peer CSX, 
despite the two companies operating similar, overlapping networks in the Eastern part of the US. 
CSX has a new management team (brought in by Hunter Harrison, now deceased) that is executing 
the precision railroading model and exposing the full margin potential of a well-run, efficient railroad. 
By 2021 CSX could report margins in the low-to-mid 40s. CSX and Norfolk Southern have 
approximately the same revenues but the market capitalisation of CSX is over 30% greater than that 
of Norfolk Southern. We think this valuation gap can close when Norfolk Southern moves to achieve 
a comparable level of profitability. 
 
Union Pacific (UP), a railroad in the Western US, competes with Burlington Northern (owned by 
Berkshire Hathaway) and has an excellent network that dominates the South West of the US. It has 
an attractive chemical franchise on the Gulf coast and a strong domestic intermodal business. 
Volumes should grow in the low single digits and due to lower than historic capex requirements (the 
Positive Train Control network has now been fully built out) UP will convert over 90% of its net 
income into free cash flow. The company is targeting 300bps of margin expansion by 2020 and UP 
will also buy back about 20% of its shares outstanding in the next two years as it utilises its under-
levered balance sheet.  
 
Canadian Pacific (CP) owns a trans-continental network in Canada and competes mainly with 
Canadian National (CN). It has large grain, intermodal, potash and energy businesses and is 
benefitting from the growth in the port of Vancouver where CP is gaining market share from CN. The 
CEO targets high single digit revenue growth in the next three years which should drive strong 
margin expansion. The company will also buy back about 3% of its shares outstanding every year. 
 
The three North American railroad stocks which we own are stable infrastructure assets trading on 
around 12x 2021 earnings and we expect them to generate IRRs in the low to mid-teens.
